## A Low-Cost Predictive Maintenance System for CO2 Laser Cutting Machines Based on Multi-Sensor Data and Supervised Machine Learning

##### Mayra Comina Tubón * , Joe Guerrero and Cristina Manobanda

Department of Energy Sciences and Mechanics, Universidad de las Fuerzas Armadas-ESPE, Sangolqui 171103, Ecuador

* Correspondence: mecomina@espe.edu.ec

##### Abstract

This study presents a structured multi-sensor predictive maintenance framework for CO2 laser cutting machines based on real-time data acquisition and supervised machine learning. The proposed architecture integrates heterogeneous sensor signals—including vibration, temperature, humidity, and acoustic measurements—through synchronized feature-level fusion to characterize machine operational states. A statistically grounded thresholding strategy, validated using two years of operational observations and controlled experimental perturbations, is employed to distinguish normal and abnormal behavior. Sensor data are processed using a Decision Tree classifier implemented in Python with Scikit-learn, enabling short-horizon probabilistic fault prediction during operational cycles. The system is deployed in a real industrial environment and validated using cross-validation and structured dataset partitioning to assess generalization performance. Results demonstrate reliable fault discrimination capability under controlled operational conditions, highlighting the effectiveness of feature-level sensor integration for early anomaly detection. The modular hardware–software architecture supports adaptability to other CNC platforms with appropriate recalibration and retraining. The proposed framework provides a lowcost, interpretable, and computationally efficient solution for real-time industrial predictive maintenance applications.

#### 1. Introduction

Laser cutting is a manufacturing technique that uses concentrated thermal energy to generate a high-intensity beam capable of melting or vaporizing material along a predefined path. This technology allows the production of flat components with high dimensional accuracy and reduced processing time, making it widely adopted in prototyping laboratories and industrial environments [1].

Laser cutting machines operate together with auxiliary subsystems that are essential for their proper performance, including chillers, blowers, and compressors. The chiller is responsible for cooling the water circulating through the CO2 laser tube, the blower extracts residual particles and contaminated air from the cutting chamber, and the compressor assists both in tube cooling and in removing dust from the working area. Failures in any of these subsystems can significantly affect cutting quality, operational safety, and equipment lifespan.

- 1.1. Predictive Maintenance

The use of advanced machinery in smart prototyping laboratories requires more sophisticated control strategies for equipment operation and maintenance. In recent years, artificial intelligence has experienced remarkable development and been widely applied in industrial environments to monitor and optimize process performance [2].

Implementing an adequate maintenance strategy is essential to preserving equipment under optimal operating conditions and to reducing operational costs. Predictive maintenance has therefore emerged as a key approach, as it relies on historical and realtime data analysis to anticipate potential failures before they result in functional breakdowns. This strategy minimizes unplanned downtime while extending the useful life of industrial machinery.

Predictive maintenance is closely related to instrumentation and artificial intelligence, since accurate fault prediction requires continuous data acquisition and detailed performance analysis of mechanical and environmental variables. Artificial intelligence enables the creation of structured databases from sensor data and supports automated decisionmaking processes, reducing operator workload and improving maintenance planning.

For this type of application, supervised machine learning models are commonly used because they facilitate data classification and fault identification when working with labeled datasets. Typical models employed in predictive maintenance include logistic regression, decision trees, and support vector machines [3].

- 1.2. State of the Art


Early applications of predictive maintenance using artificial intelligence focused on additive manufacturing systems. In [4], a preventive maintenance system for polymerbased 3D printers is developed by combining sensor data with machine learning regression models to predict the behavior of critical machine components.

Smart monitoring and maintenance of laser cutting machines are addressed in [5], where a sensor network is deployed to measure variables such as temperature, pressure, vibration, voltage, and displacement. Data are transmitted using STM32 and ESP32 microcontrollers to a cloud platform via MQTT, enabling remote monitoring, fault diagnosis, and maintenance alerts.

Sensor-based fault detection has also been extensively studied for rotating machinery. In [6], vibration data from bearings are analyzed using smart sensors to detect mechanical faults. Similarly, ref. [7] applies sensor data in metal-processing industries to develop artificial intelligence algorithms capable of estimating machinery health and supporting predictive maintenance planning.

Recent advances in sensor-based predictive systems have explored virtual sample generation and deep representation learning to enhance calibration and fault diagnosis robustness. Autoencoder-based frameworks have been proposed for in situ sensor calibration by reconstructing reference-consistent virtual samples, thereby reducing dependency on manual recalibration procedures [8]. Additionally, diffusion-based data generation techniques guided by domain knowledge have demonstrated potential in zeroshot fault diagnosis scenarios, particularly in building thermal systems and large-scale energy infrastructures [9]. These approaches aim to improve information completeness and mitigate constraints imposed by limited labeled data.

While the present study employs supervised classification using experimentally acquired real sensor measurements, the integration of representation learning and virtual sample augmentation methods represents a promising direction for enhancing generalization capability in future implementations.

Advanced signal processing and real-time monitoring techniques have been applied in safety-critical environments. In [10], a wireless sensor-based monitoring system for nuclear power plants is presented, achieving high fault detection accuracy through neural networks and Fourier-based feature extraction.

The integration of artificial intelligence, IoT, cloud data storage, and large-scale data analytics is explored in [11], where a unified predictive maintenance platform is proposed for smart manufacturing systems. The platform processes large datasets to optimize maintenance strategies across industrial assets.

Deep learning approaches have further expanded predictive maintenance capabilities. In [12], an end-to-end deep learning framework is proposed for fault detection in marine machinery using sensor data and attention-based neural architectures.

More recent studies emphasize efficiency, scalability, and interpretability. In [13], Spiking Neural Networks are applied to smart building management, demonstrating efficient real-time anomaly detection with low energy consumption. A systematic review of machine learning and OPC-UA integration for industrial automation is presented in [14], identifying predictive maintenance as a dominant research area between 2018 and 2024.

The role of artificial intelligence in Industry 4.0 and 5.0 transitions is analyzed in [15], highlighting improvements in real-time data processing, predictive maintenance, and human-centered manufacturing. Explainable deep learning models for air-compressor condition monitoring are proposed in [16], thereby improving both fault-detection accuracy and system transparency.

Low-cost predictive maintenance solutions for resource-limited IoT devices are investigated in [17], demonstrating that accurate fault detection can be achieved with constrained hardware. Hybrid artificial intelligence models for decision-making in power transformer maintenance are presented in [18], enabling equipment-specific maintenance actions in critical energy systems.

The integration of AI and IoT in manufacturing environments is addressed in [19], where ensemble machine learning models are used to predict motor temperature and vibration with high accuracy. Advanced vibration-based fault detection using variational mode decomposition and deep learning is proposed in [20], achieving superior diagnostic performance.

Edge computing solutions for industrial monitoring are analyzed in [21], demonstrating reduced inference time and energy consumption while preserving model interpretability. System robustness against physical adversarial attacks is examined in [22], highlighting the importance of resilience in AI-based fault diagnosis systems. Finally, hybrid machine learning techniques for fault detection in software tools integrated with human–computer interfaces are presented in [23].

- 1.3. Research Gap and Contribution


Despite the extensive research on artificial intelligence-based predictive maintenance, a clear gap remains in the development of low-cost, integrated systems that simultaneously monitor multiple physical variables: temperature, humidity, vibration, and noise within a complete IoT architecture and an interpretable supervised learning framework, specifically applied to CNC equipment such as laser cutting machines.

To address this gap, the present study proposes a structured multi-sensor predictive maintenance framework based on feature-level fusion. Unlike single-variable monitoring approaches, the system synchronously acquires heterogeneous sensor signals—temperature, vibration, humidity, and acoustic measurements—and transforms them into a joint feature-space representation.

Data acquisition is performed through an ESP32-based architecture communicating with a Python (version 3.8) processing environment via MQTT, enabling structured storage and real-time analysis. Rather than independently evaluating each signal, statistical descriptors extracted from temporal windows are concatenated into a unified multivariate representation that captures cross-sensor dependencies associated with incipient fault patterns.

This feature-level integration allows the supervised learning model to exploit multivariate interactions among thermal, mechanical, acoustic, and environmental variables, improving discriminative capability compared to isolated threshold-based monitoring strategies.

To address these identified gaps, the present study proposes a structured multi-sensor predictive maintenance framework. The specific scientific contributions of this work are detailed below.

- 2.3. Multi-Sensor Feature Fusion Strategy

The proposed methodology implements feature-level fusion of heterogeneous sensor signals (see Figure 2). Temperature, vibration, humidity, and acoustic measurements are synchronously acquired and segmented into fixed temporal windows. For each window, statistical descriptors are extracted per channel. These descriptors are not evaluated independently; instead, they are concatenated into a unified feature vector representing the instantaneous operational state of the machine.

The triaxial accelerometer was validated under controlled mechanical excitation. Baseline measurements were taken while the machine was idle to determine background vibration levels. Repeatability tests were conducted across multiple operational cycles.

Mechanical interference due to structural resonance and ambient vibration was evaluated. A moving-average filter was applied to smooth transient spikes prior to feature extraction.

- 2.5.3. Noise Sensor (ZTS-300BK-ZS-V05, Zhuhai ZTS Electronics Co., Ltd., Zhuhai, China)

The acoustic sensor was validated against a calibrated sound level meter under different sound intensity levels. Measurements were compared across controlled noise sources to evaluate linearity and response stability.

Ambient environmental noise was analyzed during the idle machine state to determine background baseline levels.

- • Mechanical background vibration.

- 2.6. Data Acquisition Cycle and Knowledge-Based Fault Definition


Sensor data were acquired during real-time operation of the CO2 laser cutting machine under regular production tasks. Measurements from temperature, humidity, vibration, and noise sensors were recorded at fixed time intervals and stored for offline analysis. Individual machine learning samples correspond to segmented time windows extracted from continuous monitoring sessions.

The selection of monitored variables and the definition of fault states were not arbitrarily determined. Over a two-year operational period, experienced machine operators identified recurring failure patterns associated with specific combinations of abnormal temperature rise, excessive vibration, acoustic anomalies, and environmental conditions. These empirical observations provided practical insight into the precursors of machine malfunction events.

Based on this accumulated operational knowledge, the four selected sensors were installed to capture the most relevant physical indicators associated with previously observed failure conditions.

- 2.6.1. Fault Sample Generation Fault samples in the dataset were constructed through a combination of:

- 1. Historical operator-identified fault conditions observed during real machine operation.
- 2. Controlled experimental perturbations designed to safely reproduce representative fault scenarios, such as induced motor overload and mechanical imbalance.


Each induced condition was maintained long enough to ensure a stable sensor response before recording data samples. This hybrid methodology ensured that the dataset reflects both real-world operational knowledge and controlled experimental reproducibility.

It is important to note that the dataset primarily represents short-term fault precursors rather than long-term progressive wear over multiple years.

Fault states such as motor failure and belt failure were confirmed through physical inspection, maintenance intervention reports, and operational malfunction diagnosis.

- 2.6.2. Fault Threshold Definition Strategy


Fault thresholds were defined using a hybrid empirical–experimental methodology that integrates operational experience, controlled validation, and statistical characterization.

Over a two-year operational period, experienced machine operators identified recurring malfunction patterns associated with abnormal increases in vibration amplitude and temperature. These observations were documented and used as preliminary indicators of failure precursors. The selection of monitored variables and threshold criteria was therefore grounded in real industrial experience rather than arbitrary parameter selection.

To validate these empirical findings, controlled experimental tests were conducted to safely reproduce representative abnormal conditions, including induced motor overload and mechanical imbalance. Sensor readings collected under both nominal and induced fault states were statistically analyzed to determine separation regions between operational classes.

Baseline vibration levels during stable operation exhibited a mean value of 1.87 m/s2 and a standard deviation of 0.47 m/s2. Following a statistical control-limit principle,

the vibration fault threshold was positioned beyond three standard deviations above the mean:

Threshold = µnormal + 3 · σnormal = 3.28 m/s2

where µnormal corresponds to the average value of the monitored signal during stable nominal operation, and σnormal represents the standard deviation quantifying the intrinsic variability of the system under normal working conditions.

The implemented operational threshold (3.5 m/s2) was selected slightly above this statistical limit to provide additional robustness against transient fluctuations and reduce false alarm probability. This conservative adjustment balances sensitivity to abnormal deviations with stability during normal operational variability.

The final threshold configuration, therefore, reflects a combination of empirical industrial knowledge and statistical validation within the monitored operational domain.

- 2.8.2. Data Cleaning and Preprocessing


Before model training, a preprocessing pipeline was applied to ensure dataset consistency and quality.

The following steps were implemented:

- 1. Class distribution analysis: The dataset consisted of 739 samples corresponding to normal operation and 261 samples corresponding to fault conditions. Stratified sampling was used to preserve this distribution during training and testing.
- 2. Outlier detection: Outliers were identified using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method. Values outside the range


Q1 − 1.5 · IQR, Q3 + 1.5 · IQR

were examined. Extreme measurement artifacts caused by transient noise were removed.

- 3. Sensor validation and stability verification: Prior to AI modeling, each sensor was validated through bench and in situ tests (Section 3.1). Low standard deviations and relative errors below 7% confirmed measurement reliability.
- 4. Unit consistency normalization: Although scaling was not required for Decision Tree classifiers, unit consistency was verified to prevent magnitude-related bias.


No synthetic oversampling or artificial balancing techniques were applied, as the class distribution reflected realistic operational conditions.

To compensate for transient measurement fluctuations, a moving-average filter was applied to vibration and acoustic signals before feature extraction. Baseline correction was implemented by subtracting idle-state reference values when appropriate. These steps reduced noise sensitivity without altering fault-related patterns.

All preprocessing steps were embedded within the cross-validation loop to prevent information leakage.

- 2.8.3. Cross-Validation Integrated Preprocessing Pipeline


The preprocessing workflow was integrated within the cross-validation loop to prevent information leakage between training and validation partitions. All data cleaning and transformation steps were performed exclusively on the training subset of each fold.

For each cross-validation iteration:

- - Outlier detection using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method was computed based solely on the training data.
- - Identified outlier thresholds were then applied to the validation subset without recalculating distribution statistics.
- - Baseline correction parameters were derived from training samples only.
- - Moving-average filtering was applied independently to each subset without sharing temporal statistics.


No preprocessing operations were applied globally prior to dataset partitioning. Although Decision Tree classifiers are scale-invariant and do not require feature

standardization, preprocessing steps were encapsulated within a structured Scikit-learn Pipeline to guarantee methodological rigor and reproducibility.

The implemented pipeline can be conceptually represented as follows: Pipeline = Preprocessing → Feature Vector Construction → Decision Tree Classification This approach ensures that all transformations are fitted exclusively on training data

within each fold and subsequently applied to validation data, thereby preserving statistical independence and preventing optimistic bias.

- 2.9. Fault Label Generation and Verification Protocol


Fault labels were not defined through direct thresholding of sensor measurements. Instead, the labeling process followed an independent mechanical verification protocol.

Operational fault states such as motor failure and belt failure were identified based on:

- • Observable mechanical malfunction events during machine operation;
- • Maintenance intervention reports confirming the failure source;
- • Physical inspection of components (belt wear, misalignment, motor overheating, torque loss, abnormal vibration patterns verified during maintenance).


Sensor data corresponding to these events were recorded during confirmed fault states but were not used to define the fault condition itself.

Label assignment was therefore performed after mechanical confirmation, ensuring independence from the feature extraction and model training process.

This separation between fault verification and feature-based modeling avoids circular dependency and ensures methodological rigor.

- 2.10. Predictive Horizon Definition Predictive maintenance systems can be classified according to their anticipation window:

- • Condition monitoring: detects abnormal conditions at the moment they occur.
- • Short-horizon predictive maintenance: detects degradation patterns preceding failure events.
- • Remaining Useful Life (RUL) estimation: predicts the exact time-to-failure.


The proposed system operates within the second category. Let

P(Y = k|X)

denote the predicted probability that the machine is in the failure class k given the sensor inputs X.

A warning is triggered when

P(Y = k|X) > τ

where τ is a predefined confidence threshold.

The model anticipates failure conditions within short operational horizons, typically within:

- • Several operational cycles;
- • Minutes to hours of continuous operation.


This anticipation window allows operators to intervene before mechanical damage escalates into a system shutdown.

The model does not currently estimate exact time-to-failure in hours or days. Instead,

- it predicts the likelihood of imminent failure states based on real-time degradation patterns.

- 3.3. Generalization Assessment and Overfitting Analysis


To evaluate the model’s generalization capability and mitigate overfitting risks, a structured validation strategy was implemented.

The dataset was divided using an 80–20% stratified split, ensuring proportional representation of operational and fault conditions. The test set remained completely unseen during model training and hyperparameter tuning, preventing data leakage.

Additionally, five-fold cross-validation was conducted on the training dataset. The Decision Tree classifier achieved stable performance across folds, indicating consistent decision boundaries and low variance within the dataset distribution.

The similarity between cross-validation performance and independent test-set performance suggests that the model does not exhibit memorization behavior under the controlled experimental conditions.

However, it must be noted that generalization is currently validated within the same operational domain from which the data were collected. Cross-domain validation under different materials, long-term wear progression, and varying environmental conditions remains part of future research efforts.

All preprocessing operations, including segmentation and statistical feature extraction, were performed independently within training and testing partitions to avoid information leakage. The testing dataset remained strictly isolated during model training and hyperparameter tuning.

Label assignment preceded feature extraction and model training. Sensor measurements were segmented only after fault states had been independently confirmed. Therefore, the labeling process was not influenced by the machine learning features or classification outputs.

#### 4. Discussion

After implementing the proposed system and conducting the corresponding experimental tests, a detailed analysis of the behavior of the monitored variables—noise, temperature, relative humidity, and vibration—was performed. Based on this analysis, maximum operational thresholds were established at 85 dB, 38 ◦C, 80%, and 3.5 m/s2, respectively. When any of the sensor readings exceeds these predefined limits, an anomalous condition is detected, indicating the presence of a potential fault in one or more machine components or auxiliary subsystems.

Sensor drift was indirectly evaluated through repeated validation measurements. The vibration accelerometer (ADXL345) and acoustic sensor exhibited low dispersion and stable responses during bench testing. However, long-term calibration drift may occur under extended industrial operation. Continuous recalibration strategies may therefore be required in large-scale deployment scenarios.

Although acceleration amplitude proved sufficient for distinguishing induced fault conditions within the controlled dataset, advanced vibration diagnostics in industrial environments often rely on frequency-domain analysis (e.g., Fast Fourier Transform). Incorporating spectral features could improve sensitivity to bearing faults, misalignment, and resonance phenomena. Therefore, future work will integrate frequency-based indicators to enhance diagnostic resolution.

Considering the accuracy and precision of each sensing device, it can be concluded that the sensor layer operates reliably and provides data of sufficient quality for training artificial intelligence models. This assessment was further validated using the Shapiro– Wilk normality test, which yielded p-values greater than 0.89 for all variables. These results indicate that the datasets follow a normal distribution and are therefore suitable for statistical analysis and supervised learning techniques.

Although the system does not compute long-term Remaining Useful Life (RUL), it anticipates fault states before complete breakdown occurs. During experimental testing, anomalous patterns were detected prior to operational instability, allowing corrective action before damage propagation.

Future work will focus on integrating degradation trend analysis and survival modeling techniques to estimate time-to-failure in extended temporal horizons.

The evaluated artificial intelligence models exhibited excellent performance. Through analysis of accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score, it was determined that both the Decision Tree and Support Vector Machine models achieved ideal performance on the evaluated dataset, with 100% accuracy and precision and complete elimination of Type I and Type II errors. However, due to its operational characteristics, the Decision Tree model provides faster inference and requires lower computational resources, making it more suitable for real-time industrial applications and embedded system deployment.

Although the classification models achieved perfect performance within the experimental dataset, this outcome must be interpreted within the context of controlled laboratory conditions. The induced fault scenarios were generated under predefined operational states with limited environmental variability and short-term data acquisition. In real industrial environments, additional factors such as long-term mechanical degradation, material variability, ambient noise fluctuations, and sensor calibration drift may increase class overlap and reduce predictive performance. Therefore, large-scale longitudinal validation under real production conditions is required to fully evaluate generalization capacity.

Figure 12 illustrates the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve obtained for the Decision Tree classifier. The curve reaches the upper-left corner of the ROC space, achieving an Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 1.00. The Shapiro–Wilk test was conducted as part of a descriptive statistical analysis to characterize the dataset distribution. Although normality is not required for Decision Tree or Support Vector Machine classifiers, this analysis provided additional insight into data behavior prior to modeling.

Once the system was fully deployed, a predictive maintenance plan for the laser cutting machine was successfully established. By continuously analyzing sensor data, the algorithm detects potential faults and triggers visual alarms on the Human–Machine Interface (HMI), indicating the need for inspection or maintenance of the subsystem responsible for the anomalous behavior. This early warning capability helps prevent severe equipment damage, reduces material waste by anticipating improper operation, and contributes to optimized machine performance and improved cut quality.

Overall, the obtained results demonstrate that the proposed approach effectively integrates sensing, data analysis, and artificial intelligence to support reliable and practical predictive maintenance in laser cutting systems. The combination of interpretable models, low computational requirements, and real-time monitoring makes the solution particularly suitable for deployment in laboratory and small-to-medium industrial environments.

While the present study does not include multi-month longitudinal validation under full industrial variability, the deployment on an operational laser cutter confirms feasibility in real equipment conditions. Extended long-term validation campaigns, including heterogeneous materials and continuous production shifts, are required to quantify economic savings and reliability improvements with higher precision.

Although the current validation framework demonstrates robustness on unseen samples drawn from the same distribution, domain shift scenarios may affect classification performance. Industrial environments with extended operating cycles, material variability, and progressive mechanical degradation may introduce feature distribution changes. Future work will incorporate extended longitudinal datasets and cross-domain validation to evaluate robustness under broader industrial variability.

- 4.1. Threshold Robustness and Adaptation Perspectives


The current implementation employs fixed thresholds derived from empirical observation and statistical validation. While this approach demonstrated stable behavior under controlled operational conditions, industrial systems are subject to domain shifts caused by gradual mechanical wear, environmental variability, and material heterogeneity.

Future research will explore dynamic threshold adjustment mechanisms based on rolling statistical windows, adaptive control limits, or residual-based anomaly scoring. Such approaches may enhance robustness under long-term degradation scenarios and varying production conditions.

Nevertheless, the present fixed-threshold strategy provides a transparent, interpretable, and computationally efficient solution suitable for real-time deployment in resource-constrained industrial environments.

- 4.2. Portability and Industrial Adaptation The portability of the proposed system relies on three main components:


- 1. Sensor modularity: Sensors can be reconfigured based on machine-specific failure modes.
- 2. Data-driven retraining: The Decision Tree classifier can be retrained using new labeled datasets corresponding to different CNC equipment.
- 3. Threshold recalibration: Statistical characterization of nominal operation must be repeated for each new machine to define appropriate control limits.

While cross-machine validation was not performed in the present study, the system architecture does not depend on laser-specific parameters, suggesting strong potential for adaptation in broader industrial scenarios.

- 4.3. Multi-Sensor Integration Perspective

The implemented multi-sensor framework does not rely on simple parallel monitoring. Instead, it constructs a joint feature representation that captures cross-sensor dependencies. For instance, vibration anomalies combined with temperature drift may indicate mechanical degradation, while isolated variations might correspond only to transient fluctuations. This multivariate representation enhances discriminative capability compared to isolated sensor monitoring strategies.

- 5. Conclusions This work presented the design, implementation, and experimental validation of a


predictive maintenance system for a CO2 laser cutting machine, based on multi-sensor data acquisition, an IoT communication workflow, and supervised machine learning. The system continuously monitors temperature, relative humidity, vibration, and noise, enabling the identification of anomalous operating conditions associated with machine components.

The sensing layer exhibited stable behavior under laboratory conditions. Validation tests yielded relative errors below 7% and low dispersion levels, confirming that the measurements obtained are suitable for condition monitoring and subsequent data-driven analysis. Furthermore, the Shapiro–Wilk test resulted in p-values exceeding 0.89 for all variables, indicating statistical consistency of the dataset for supervised learning.

Three supervised classifiers were evaluated for fault detection. The Decision Tree and Support Vector Machine (SVM) models achieved perfect classification on the evaluated dataset, with zero false positives or false negatives. The Decision Tree model was selected for implementation due to its low computational complexity, fast inference, and direct interpretability, which are advantageous for real-time monitoring and embedded implementation.

Although this study was validated on a CO2 laser cutting machine, the proposed predictive maintenance framework is inherently modular and adaptable. The architecture integrates generic physical monitoring variables—vibration, temperature, acoustic signals, and environmental conditions—that are relevant across a broad range of CNC equipment.

The hardware layer can be adapted to other industrial machines by selecting sensors aligned with machine-specific critical components. Similarly, the machine learning module can be retrained using operational data from alternative CNC platforms, enabling domain adaptation without structural modification of the predictive algorithm.

Therefore, the methodology is not restricted to laser cutting systems and can be extended to milling machines, lathes, plasma cutting equipment, or other automated manufacturing systems, provided appropriate recalibration and dataset collection are performed.

From a practical standpoint, the proposed approach provides an interpretable and costeffective solution that combines real-time detection, database storage, and alarm generation via a Graphical User Interface (GUI). This integration facilitates the timely execution of maintenance actions, contributes to reducing unscheduled downtime, and helps preserve operational quality by detecting abnormal behavior before it becomes critical.

While the model demonstrated perfect accuracy within the controlled experimental domain, the system has not yet been validated across multiple industrial facilities or longterm degradation cycles. Therefore, the reported performance should be interpreted as domain-specific rather than universally generalizable.
