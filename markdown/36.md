Unexpected equipment failures in industrial systems can lead to signiﬁcant production downtime, increased operational costs, and reduced asset life cycles. Leveraging recent advancements in data science and intelligent decision-support systems, this paper proposes an explainable data-driven optimization and mathematical modeling framework for predictive maintenance scheduling in industrial environments. The framework integrates sensor-based condition monitoring data with machine learning models to forecast potential equipment failures before they occur. A supervised learning approach, implemented using gradient boosting and temporal feature engineering, predicts the remaining useful life (RUL) and failure probability of critical assets. An explainability layer, based on SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values, provides interpretable insights into the most inﬂuential factors contributing to predicted failures, enabling maintenance engineers to validate model outputs and trust automated recommendations. The predictive outputs are then embedded into a mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) model to generate optimal maintenance schedules that minimize total downtime and maintenance costs while satisfying operational constraints such as resource availability and production deadlines. The proposed framework is validated using a combination of publicly available predictive maintenance datasets and real-world industrial sensor data. Experimental results demonstrate a reduction of up to 22% in unplanned downtime and 15% in maintenance costs compared to reactive and preventive maintenance strategies, while maintaining high interpretability for domain experts. This integrated approach highlights the potential of combining explainable AI, predictive analytics, and mathematical optimization for sustainable and eﬃcient industrial operations.

- 1.1. Problem context and statement

This work studies the end-to-end pipeline that links prognostics to maintenance execution. We consider a set of assets indexed by 𝑖 ∈  monitored over discrete periods 𝑡 ∈  with multivariate sensor streams and historical maintenance logs. The learning task is to produce timedependent predictions of (i) RUL, denoted 𝑟̂𝑖(𝑡), and (ii) the probability that asset 𝑖 fails within a look-ahead window 𝜏, denoted 𝑝̂𝑖(𝑡,𝜏). These predictions are then used to plan maintenance start times, task types, and crew allocations over the horizon  so as to minimize a weighted combination of expected unplanned downtime and direct maintenance costs. The schedule must satisfy practical constraints, including crew and tooling capacities, non-overlap of tasks on the same asset, access and safety windows, and interactions with production campaigns. The core diﬃculty is twofold: predictive uncertainty and potential miscalibration can distort perceived risk, and the mapping from probabilistic risk to optimization penalties is often ad hoc, obscuring traceability between forecasts and decisions.

- 1.2. Proposed framework

We propose an explainable data-driven optimization and mathematical modeling framework that couples calibrated prognostics with mixedinteger linear programming (MILP). On the prediction side, we adopt supervised learning with gradient-boosted decision trees and time-aware feature engineering to model degradation dynamics and near-term failure likelihoods [4,5]. Strict temporal splits and probability calibration are used to reduce look-ahead bias and improve reliability under deployment shift. To make the learned signals auditable, we incorporate an explainability layer based on SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), providing global importance proﬁles and local attributions that expose which sensor trends, lags, and rolling statistics most strongly drive risk at a given time [6]. On the decision side, we embed the predictive outputs into an MILP that schedules preventive and corrective actions while respecting all operational constraints typical of industrial settings [7]. The coupling is explicit: predicted RUL inﬂuences soft deadlines and tardiness penalties; calibrated failure probabilities shape risk-weighted costs; and SHAP-identiﬁed drivers guide priority rules when resources are scarce. A rolling-horizon control loop updates predictions as new telemetry arrives and re-optimizes the schedule, thus aligning plan responsiveness with the pace of asset degradation.

- 1.3. Research questions and hypotheses

The study evaluates four questions central to trustworthy PdM scheduling. First, we ask how accurately gradient-boosted models with temporally engineered features can predict RUL and short-horizon failure risk under time-ordered validation. Second, we examine the stability and actionability of SHAP-based explanations across assets and time, and how these diagnostics support model acceptance and policy reﬁnement on the shop ﬂoor. Third, we quantify how an explicit, auditable mapping from predictive signals to MILP objectives and constraints aﬀects realized downtime, cost, and feasibility relative to reactive and time-based preventive baselines. Fourth, we analyze robustness: we stress-test the pipeline under forecast errors, constrained resources, and tight production deadlines, and measure the beneﬁt of rolling-horizon replanning in recovering performance.

- 1.5. Scope and signiﬁcance

By unifying explainable prognostics with mathematical programming, the framework moves beyond black-box or siloed approaches. It supports auditability, engineers can inspect why a particular intervention was recommended; adaptability, rolling-horizon updates align plans with evolving degradation; and feasibility, explicit constraints encode operational realities. - 2.6. Positioning of our work


Predictive maintenance aims to anticipate equipment failures by leveraging historical and real-time sensor data to estimate remaining useful life (RUL) or failure risk [1,14–17]. Traditional PdM methods rely on physics-based degradation models or statistical approaches [18], whereas contemporary eﬀorts increasingly adopt machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) frameworks, exploiting complex, multivariate telemetry [19,20]. Techniques include supervised regression and classiﬁcation [21], survival analysis for hazard estimation [22], and anomaly detection-based schemes [23]. Temporal feature extraction and sensor fusion remain crucial for accurate and robust predictions [24], often implemented with sliding or rolling window methods [25]. Recent advancements also explore hybrid sensor network topologies that enhance data collection reliability and energy eﬃciency, thereby supporting sustained, high-quality data streams critical for eﬀective PdM [26]. Recent advances in RUL prediction have focused on deep learning approaches and ensemble methods to improve accuracy and robustness [27–29].

The intersection of predictive maintenance, explainable AI, and optimization has resulted in integrated frameworks capable of producing actionable, trustworthy, and resource-aware maintenance schedules. Despite signiﬁcant advances, challenges remain in scalability, uncertainty quantiﬁcation, causal inference, and real-time responsiveness. This work builds on these foundations by proposing a uniﬁed framework that tightly couples reliable data-driven predictions, interpretable explanations, and robust MILP-based scheduling under realistic operational constraints. As highlighted in Table 1, our framework uniquely addresses the integration gaps in existing approaches by providing explicit traceability between SHAP-based explanations and optimization decisions, incorporating uncertainty-aware scheduling, and validating across multiple industrial sectors. Unlike prior works that treat prediction, explanation, and optimization as separate components, our approach establishes a seamless coupling where explainability directly informs decision-making, enabling auditable and adaptive maintenance scheduling in industrial environments.

### 3. Data and problem context

This section speciﬁes the operational setting and sensing modalities, details the public benchmarks and the Lebanese industrial corpus used for validation, deﬁnes the learning targets consumed by the predictive layer and the decision parameters required by the scheduler, and documents the preprocessing and temporal feature engineering steps that transform raw telemetry and maintenance logs into analysis-ready panels.

3.1. Assets, sensors, and operating regimes

The study considers a heterogeneous ﬂeet of production assets 𝑖 ∈  operating under time-varying regimes 𝑡 ∈  and instrumented with multi-rate sensing. Rotating equipment such as motors, pumps, fans, compressors, bearings, and gearboxes are monitored by tri-axial accelerometers for high-frequency vibration and envelope signals, complemented by thermocouples or RTDs for temperature and electrical transducers for current, voltage, power factor. Process equipment contributes pressure, ﬂow, level, and quality analyzers sampled via the plant historian or DCS/SCADA. Sampling spans sub-second to minute scales: vibration snapshots are acquired at 12-25kHz in one- to two-second bursts at regular intervals or on threshold triggers; process and electrical channels are typically recorded every one to ten seconds. Exogenous and endogenous operating conditions, line speed, load and torque, upstream/downstream buﬀers, ambient temperature and humidity, product mix, and shift patterns, modulate both levels and variability of signals are encoded as regime descriptors 𝑟𝑖(𝑡) to control for confounding and to support stratiﬁed validation. Maintenance interventions produce structural breaks in the telemetry; to preserve stationarity within episodes, each asset’s timeline is segmented into intervals bounded by interventions and major conﬁguration changes, and labels and features are computed within those stable segments.

Combining ML predictions with mathematical optimization to close the prediction-to-action gap has gained prominence [48]. Frameworks integrate calibrated failure probabilities or RUL metrics as parameters or constraints within scheduling MILPs [2,49]. Risk-informed cost functions and robust optimization techniques ensure solutions withstand predictive uncertainty [50,51]. Rolling-horizon and decomposition methods improve scalability for large-scale industrial scenarios [52,53]. Hybrid solution methods merging heuristics, metaheuristics, and exact solvers address combinatorial complexity [54,55]. Despite these advances, existing frameworks often lack explicit traceability between predictive insights and optimization decisions, limiting their adoption in safety-critical industrial environments [56].

The Lebanese context introduces distinctive operating regimes and data considerations that are explicitly modeled. Electrical supply intermittency and generator switchover events can induce voltage sags, transient harmonics, and short production halts; these are represented as exogenous regime ﬂags and are ﬁltered from degradation signals to avoid spurious correlations. The result is a multi-site, multisector Lebanese corpus that is representative of regional operational realities while meeting reproducibility and privacy constraints.

- 3.5. Preprocessing and temporal feature engineering


### 4. Predictive modeling

Preprocessing harmonizes clocks, removes artifacts, and constructs causal, informative features while respecting the chronology required for deployment. Time alignment reconciles historian and edge-device stamps; low-frequency channels are forward-ﬁlled within physically justiﬁed hold intervals and are otherwise interpolated conservatively, whereas high-frequency vibration snapshots are anchored at acquisition times and treated as independent frames. Data quality procedures include: (1) Automated validation checks for sensor ranges, spike detection, and communication errors; (2) Manual veriﬁcation by domain experts for ambiguous cases; (3) Regular calibration of sensors against reference standards. Instrument resets, saturations, and communication glitches are identiﬁed by spike detection and ﬂag patterns and are winsorized or excised depending on physical plausibility. Oﬀset jumps are detected with cumulative sum change-point tests; slow thermal or seasonal drifts are de-trended when separable from degradation. For vibration, each frame is de-meaned and tapered before feature extraction in time and frequency domains. Extracted descriptors include root-meansquare, variance, skewness, kurtosis, crest and shape factors, bandlimited energies, spectral centroid and bandwidth, spectral kurtosis, envelope spectra near bearing characteristic frequencies, and cepstral coeﬃcients; in cement and dusty environments, band selection is adapted to the measured noise ﬂoor. For propulsion-like telemetry, health indices combine temperature ratios, pressure diﬀerentials, and normalized fuel or power ﬂow; for process sensors, features include lagged diﬀerences, exponentially weighted moving averages, rolling quantiles, Z-normalized control statistics, and residuals from local linear or autoregressive models to isolate deviations from regime baselines. Generator switchover grid perturbation events are marked as exogenous regime variables and are either excluded from training windows or modeled explicitly to prevent the learner from mistaking power-quality transients for incipient mechanical defects.

The empirical results show that calibrated, explainable prognostics coupled with a transparent MILP scheduler yield material operational gains while preserving auditability. In practical terms for maintenance planners, the pipeline turns continuous streams of heterogeneous signals into prioritized, time-stamped work orders backed by explicit rationales. The calibrated short-horizon risks focus attention on assets that are both likely and timely to fail, while conservative RUL quantiles translate into defensible service due dates that can be negotiated against production needs. Explanatory artifacts based on SHAP values make these recommendations actionable: planners can see which vibration bands, thermal ratios, or process residuals are driving a speciﬁc alert, compare them with historical precursors on the same line, and decide whether to advance, defer, or bundle the job. When the schedule is committed through the CMMS, the same explanations travel with the work order and, post hoc, enable a traceable audit of why an intervention was recommended at that time.

There are regimes where the approach may underperform. Assets whose dominant failure modes leave weak or no signatures in the instrumented channels will challenge any data-driven prognostics; for such cases, physics-based models or additional sensing may be necessary. Highly intermittent exogenous disturbances, generator switchover artifacts, abrupt product changes, or cleaning operations, can mimic degradation unless carefully encoded as regimes and ﬁltered upstream. Plants with extremely sparse maintenance windows or chronic crew bottlenecks can force the optimizer into frequent deadline violations regardless of predictive quality, in which case the planner must either relax windows, add capacity, or accept higher risk. Integration costs are nontrivial: historian and CMMS connectors must be hardened; sensor quality procedures and time alignment need to be enforced; a model registry and calibration monitor must be operated; solver licenses and orchestration need to be maintained; and staﬀ across maintenance, production, and reliability engineering require training.

- 9.3. Limitations

This work has several important limitations that must be acknowledged. First, the framework relies on historical labels and may be vulnerable to dataset shift in dynamic industrial environments. While our rolling-horizon approach mitigates this to some extent, signiﬁcant changes in operating conditions or failure patterns could degrade performance. Second, while SHAP provides associational attributions, it does not establish causal relationships between sensor patterns and failures. The risk-shaping factor 𝜅𝑖,𝑡 should be interpreted as increasing the penalty for risk signals that are associated with known degradation patterns, not as a causal mechanism. Third, the Lebanese industrial dataset cannot be shared due to conﬁdentiality agreements, limiting reproducibility of the full industrial case study. Fourth, computational scalability may be challenged for very large-scale deployments with hundreds of assets, though our results show the approach remains feasible for medium-sized facilities. Finally, the framework assumes relatively stable maintenance durations and resource availability, which may not hold in highly dynamic environments with frequent disruptions.

- 9.4. Comparison with State-of-the-Art

Compared to recent integrated PdM frameworks, our approach offers several distinct advantages. Unlike methods that treat prediction, explanation, and optimization as separate components [2,49], we establish explicit traceability between SHAP-based explanations and MILP parameters. This enables auditable decision-making that was previously lacking in black-box approaches. While recent works have explored uncertainty-aware scheduling [51], they often lack the explainability component needed for operational trust. Our framework uniquely combines calibrated uncertainty quantiﬁcation with interpretable explanations and transparent optimization, creating a comprehensive solution that addresses both technical and human factors in industrial PdM deployment.

- 9.1. Practical implications and industrial adoption


The industrial case study demonstrates the framework’s readiness for real-world deployment. Maintenance engineers reported that SHAPbased explanations signiﬁcantly improved their trust in automated recommendations, as they could verify that interventions were triggered by physically meaningful signals rather than spurious correlations. The ability to trace each scheduling decision back to speciﬁc sensor patterns and risk assessments proved valuable for both operational decision-making and compliance documentation. In safety-critical environments, this audit trail supports regulatory requirements for transparent decision-making processes. The framework’s modular architecture allows for incremental adoption, starting with predictive monitoring and gradually adding optimization components as organizational maturity increases.

The main trade-oﬀ lies along the axes of accuracy, interpretability, and runtime. More expressive forecasters, temporal deep networks or hybrid survival models, can improve discrimination on certain assets, but they often complicate explanation and raise the burden of calibration under covariate shift. We deliberately favored histogram-based gradient boosting because it oﬀers competitive accuracy, reliable TreeSHAP explanations, and stable calibration with low-latency inference. On the optimization side, time-indexed MILP models are transparent and amenable to warm starts and valid inequalities, yet their runtime grows with discretization granularity, horizon length, and resource coupling. We controlled this by precomputing linear risk weights upstream, keeping the MILP purely linear, and limiting each replan to a modest time budget; the resulting optimality gaps were subpercent while solve times remained within tens of seconds per epoch, which is adequate for hourly replanning. Introducing explicit risk measures (e.g., CVaR) or chance constraints improves tail protection but lengthens solve times; the data in Section 8 suggest that simple, transparent hedges based on calibrated probabilities and lower RUL quantiles already capture much of the beneﬁt.

- 10.1. Contributions and impact

Our work makes three distinct contributions to the ﬁeld of predictive maintenance. First, we establish a novel architectural integration that explicitly couples SHAP-based explainability with MILP optimization, enabling traceable decision-making previously lacking in black-box predict-then-optimize frameworks. Second, we introduce methodological innovations in stability-tested temporal explainability and conformalized uncertainty quantiﬁcation that provide reliable risk assessment under operational variability. Third, we demonstrate empirical validation across public benchmarks and multi-sector industrial data, proving the framework’s practical feasibility and scalability. The 22% reduction in unplanned downtime and 15% cost reduction achieved in real industrial settings provide compelling evidence of the framework’s operational value.

The framework is deliberately modular. Histogram-based gradient boosting supplied accurate, low-latency forecasts that support stable TreeSHAP explanations and reliable calibration; the optimization layer remained purely linear by precomputing risk weights and due dates, which kept solve times within seconds per replan and gaps below one percent. This separation of concerns simpliﬁes governance: models, calibrators, explainers, and MILP instances are versioned artifacts with full provenance, enabling end-to-end audits of any schedule produced by the system. Although the evaluation focused on rotating machinery and process equipment, the interface is method- and domain-agnostic; the same coupling applies to ﬂeets with diﬀerent physics, provided that risk estimates are calibrated and that operational windows and costs are speciﬁed with comparable ﬁdelity.

- 10.2. Limitations


Construct validity hinges on whether the chosen targets and metrics capture the operational constructs of interest. Short-horizon failure indicators align with schedulable reaction times, and RUL is deﬁned with domain-validated thresholds; however, some industries care more about quality excursions or energy penalties than about binary failure. We partially addressed this by reporting multiple scheduling KPIs, unplanned downtime, cost, deadline compliance, utilization, makespan, and by allowing objective weights to reﬂect local economics. Statistical conclusion validity can be aﬀected by serial correlation in time series and by the small number of rare failure events. Our conﬁdence intervals and paired tests were computed on time-aligned, per-asset deltas to reduce dependence, but residual autocorrelation may still narrow intervals spuriously. Future work could incorporate block bootstrap schemes tuned to asset-speciﬁc correlation lengths.

On the optimization side, validity threats include discretization error from time indexing, the use of big-𝑀 linearisations in a few optional constraints, and the reliance on incumbent solutions when time limits are reached. We bounded discretization error by choosing granular epochs relative to job durations and veriﬁed that tighter grids did not change decisions materially on a representative subset. Big-𝑀 constants were set to tight horizon-based bounds, and reported gaps remained well below one percent. Finally, feedback eﬀects are possible: once schedules shift interventions earlier, the observed failure rate can decline, which feeds back into base-rate calibration. The rolling-horizon protocol and continuous recalibration mitigate this drift, but a full causal analysis would require counterfactual designs that are beyond the scope of the present study.

Additionally, the non-public nature of the Lebanese industrial dataset limits external reproducibility, though we have provided synthetic data with similar statistical properties and detailed methodology to enable replication. The framework’s reliance on high-quality sensor data also poses a validity threat in environments with poor instrumentation or frequent sensor failures, which we addressed through robust preprocessing and missing data protocols as described in Section 3.

Despite the demonstrated success, our framework has several important limitations that must be acknowledged. First, the approach relies on historical failure labels and may be vulnerable to dataset shift in dynamic industrial environments, particularly when operating conditions change signiﬁcantly. Second, while SHAP provides associational attributions, it does not establish causal relationships between sensor patterns and failures, which limits the framework’s ability to predict novel failure modes. Third, the Lebanese industrial dataset cannot be shared due to conﬁdentiality agreements, limiting reproducibility of the full industrial case study. Fourth, computational scalability may be challenged for very large-scale deployments with hundreds of assets, though our results show the approach remains feasible for medium-sized facilities. Finally, the framework assumes relatively stable maintenance durations and resource availability, which may not hold in highly dynamic environments with frequent disruptions. These limitations provide clear directions for future research and development.

Despite these limitations, the combination of temporal holdouts, cross-site evaluations, ablations, and sensitivity analyses provides converging evidence that the observed gains are robust and attributable to the integrated, explainable learning-optimization pipeline rather than to incidental artifacts of any single component or dataset.
