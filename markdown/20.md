In the automotive scenario, automatic real-time tyre identiﬁcation is paramount to optimize vehicle performance, enhance safety, and reduce consumption. On the one hand, this capability is valuable in racing applications. In fact, although tyre speciﬁcations are typically known a priori, recognition can provide consistency checks and strategic decisions. On the other hand, it becomes even more critical in urban scenarios, including aftermarket applications, where tyre information is often unavailable and vehicle control systems can beneﬁt from adapting their response in real time to improve stability and safety, especially during critical maneuvers. However, a clear gap has been identiﬁed in the literature on tyre classiﬁcation. In fact, most existing approaches focus on usage monitoring, such as tyre wear estimation while those explicitly addressing tyre type prediction are still lacking. Moreover, even among the existing wear estimation approaches, a trade-oﬀ emerges between practicality and interpretability; a challenge that tyre classiﬁcation methods are also expected to face. In fact, on the one hand, model-based techniques yield physically interpretable results, but typically depend on measurements that are rarely available in real-world settings, such as tyre pressure and temperature. On the other hand, machine learning approaches exploit easily accessible vehicle data and achieve high predictive performance at the expense of interpretability.

Keywords: Tyre classiﬁcation Model-based Machine learning Data-driven

To address this challenge, two approaches for real-time tyre classiﬁcation are presented and compared: a model-based method tailored to use more accessible vehicle measurements, and a learning-based approach designed with a focus on interpretability. To evaluate their performance, both a simulation scenario and real-world data are considered. While both models achieve optimal performance in simulation, data variability and noise in real-world conditions reduce the accuracy of the model-based approach. In contrast, the learning-based approach maintains an F1-score of 96.5% proving to be a practical and interpretable solution for real-time tyre recognition.

To bridge the gap in the existing literature, the present work addresses a diﬀerent but equally critical task and largely unexplored problem: the real-time recognition of the mounted tyre type. This problem is particularly relevant in high-performance or racing contexts as well as urban scenarios, where the installed tyre type directly aﬀects vehicle dynamics and control strategy. To this end this work presents and compares two novel methodologies for real-time tyre type classiﬁcation: a model-based and a machine learning-based approach. The two proposed classiﬁcation strategies are conceived with complementary roles. The model-based approach is designed as a simple, physically interpretable, and computationally eﬃcient reference method, providing a meaningful benchmark for tyre classiﬁcation based on lateral dynamics. The main methodological contribution of this work is instead represented by the learning-based strategy, which enables reliable tyre identiﬁcation when lateral tyre-related quantities are not directly available. The proposed model-based method improves on existing techniques by estimating hard-to-access variables using measurements more likely to be available, at least in high-performance vehicles. The machine learningbased method, on the other hand, has been ad-hoc designed to ensure interpretability and leverages standard in-vehicle signals. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, this is the ﬁrst study to present and compare approaches for real-time tyre classiﬁcation. Both strategies are evaluated on simulated and experimental data, to assess their feasibility and robustness in a controlled and a real-world condition, respectively. The results demonstrate both methods’ eﬀectiveness in real-time tyre classiﬁcation using simulated data. However, when applied to experimental data, the machine learning approach proves to be more robust, maintaining high performance, while the model-based one reduces its classiﬁcation capabilities. Overall, this preliminary analysis conﬁrms the validity of both approaches, yet highlights the machine learning method as more promising due to its robustness and reliance on more accessible vehicle data. classiﬁer is explicitly tailored to operate only when the lateral vehicle dynamics is suﬃciently excited. In these conditions, the underlying model equations are valid and lateral tyre forces can be reliably estimated, enabling physics-based tyre classiﬁcation. Accordingly, the model-based approach is applied only within operating regimes that satisfy predeﬁned excitation and validity conditions, while outside these regimes the classiﬁer output is intentionally held constant to avoid unreliable or noisy decisions. In the simulation scenario, full-length track trials are available, allowing the behaviour of the model-based classiﬁer to be analysed over the entire manoeuvre while explicitly highlighting the time intervals that meet the excitation and model-validity requirements. In contrast, in the experimental scenario, the available dataset consists only of selected track segments. These segments inherently correspond to portions of the manoeuvre characterized by increasing lateral forces and suﬃcient excitation, and are therefore directly used for the analysis. Conversely, the learning-based approach is evaluated in a fully data-driven fashion. No explicit assumptions on excitation levels or model validity are imposed a priori, and the entire available dataset is provided as input to the classiﬁer. This choice reﬂects the objective of assessing whether a learning-based strategy can autonomously extract discriminative information from standard vehicle signals, without requiring explicit intervention or operating-condition supervision. Feature extraction on these signals subset was conducted by exploiting a moving window approach to ensure that the classiﬁcation algorithm predictions are generated in real-time. The window width is a critical parameter, which should be determined through a ﬁne-tuning process that balances the included signal dynamics and the time required for classiﬁcation. In this case, an optimal width of 𝑤 = 50𝑚𝑠 was determined for both the simulated and the real-world scenarios. Thus, each window is an instance for the classiﬁer and contains a synchronous and ﬁxed portion of the signals of interest over which features are extracted. More in detail, a total of 12 features are computed, including the mean value, standard deviation, and mean of the ﬁrst derivative for 𝐴𝑦, 𝐴𝑥, and longitudinal slip 𝜆 for the front left wheel and front right wheel. These features were selected through a feature selection process, as they were found to be the most informative for separating the target classes while minimizing redundancy. The produced instances set is then used to train and evaluate a Random Forest Classiﬁer. This classiﬁer has been selected to further enhance interpretability (Breiman, 2001). To this end, ensemble size was also considered during hyperparameter tuning, along with prediction accuracy. This led to an optimal Random Forest model consisting of 5 estimators, with a maximum depth 10. Such a result enhances interpretability, as the decision rules of trees can be examined and also understood, as they are based on the extracted physics-based features. To train this model, the analysis relies on the dataset presented in Section 2, using 70% for training and the remainder for evaluating the performance. Once trained, the classiﬁer can be used online by ﬁlling a window with the required signals, extracting the corresponding features, and providing the derived instance to the model, which provides the tyre type prediction.

- 6.1. Results overview

Thus, it is possible to conclude that the presented results demonstrate that, while the model-based approach oﬀers high interpretability, it still depends on signals, e.g., lateral forces and side-slip angles, that are not commonly available in most production vehicles. Moreover, its performance in real-world scenarios is limited by sensitivity of estimated variables to measurement noise. In contrast, the machine learning-based approach, though inherently less interpretable than the model-based one, was speciﬁcally designed for explainability. In fact, it consists of a small forest built on physics-informed features. This conﬁguration not only ensures interpretability but also proves strong performance in both simulated and experimental scenarios, relying solely on readily available in-vehicle signals.
