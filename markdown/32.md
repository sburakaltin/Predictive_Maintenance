## A B S T R A C T

## A R T I C L E I N F O

To prevent costly failures and unplanned downtime, predictive maintenance for industrial machinery requires accurate forecasting and early anomaly recognition. This paper introduces a novel zero-cost Differentiable Neural Architecture Search framework for Vibration Analysis (DNAS-VA) that simultaneously optimizes forecasting and anomaly detection in vibration signals. The proposed approach automatically discovers the most appropriate neural network architectures by exploring a search space combining time and frequencydomain operations, including Fourier and wavelet transforms, attention mechanisms, and temporal modeling components. A Forecasting-Integrated Variational Autoencoder (FI-VAE) enhances anomaly detection by combining reconstruction error, latent space analysis, and temporal pattern assessment. The methodology employs a hierarchical training protocol to optimize both architecture search and anomaly detection performance. Experiments in real triaxial vibration data from an industrial motor demonstrate the framework’s effectiveness. The discovered architecture achieves superior forecasting performance, with mean absolute errors of 0.118–0.156 across vibration axes, and robust anomaly detection, outperforming baseline methods like Isolation Forest. Main innovations include a multi-fidelity evaluation strategy using zero-cost metrics, such as Fisher Information, correlation equal 0.90, to efficiently identify high-performing architectures without full training cycles. Latent space analysis reveals interpretable clusters corresponding to operational states, with anomalies detected at cluster boundaries. The results show that the integrated framework significantly improves predictive maintenance by enabling accurate forecasting and reliable early fault detection while reducing computational costs. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance in both tasks, offering a scalable solution for industrial condition monitoring.

### 2. Related works

Many methods have been developed to extract signals from vibration measurements. Among the most widely used are time-frequency representation techniques, including the short-time Fourier transform and the wavelet transform. Additionally, empirical mode decomposition has proven effective in signal analysis (Zheng et al., 2021). The Wigner-Ville distribution offers high-resolution time-frequency analysis, though it may suffer from cross-term interference (ClimenteAlarcon et al., 2012). For tracking time-varying velocity in noisy environments, Kalman filtering and recursive least squares are highly effective (Ding et al., 2016). Additionally, deep learning models, such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs), have demonstrated strong potential in extracting velocityrelated patterns directly from vibration data (Jing et al., 2017).

This paper introduces a novel approach that simultaneously addresses forecasting and anomaly detection for vibration data in electrical machines (Hendrickx et al., 2020). The proposed Differentiable Neural Architecture Search Framework for Vibration Analysis (DNASVA) automatically discovers optimal neural network architectures that integrate time and frequency-domain processing, while the ForecastingIntegrated Variational Autoencoder (FI-VAE) provides robust anomaly detection capabilities (Cai et al., 2023). This combined framework leverages differentiable architecture to identify the most effective components and connections for vibration forecasting, exploring a search space that includes both time and frequency domain operations. The search space encompasses various spectral transformations, including Fourier and Wavelet-based operations (Priyadarshini et al., 2024), attention mechanisms, and temporal modeling components, enabling the discovery of architectures that optimally capture both local features and global spectral characteristics of vibration patterns.

Short-time Fourier transform was used to calculate the timefrequency distribution and a new velocity estimation method based on vibration signals, which takes advantage of the concept of region of interest selection around any of the signal components visible in the time-frequency representation (Dziedziech et al., 2018). An autoregressive moving average model coupled with data-driven techniques to provide the forecast for predictive maintenance of a critical valve of an aircraft engine was used by Baptista et al. (2018).

In early applications of machine learning for vibration analysis, Zhang et al. (2015) applied the Support Vector Machines (SVM) to classify faults from vibration signals, although this approach used a relatively shallow architecture compared to more recent methodologies (Brusamarello et al., 2023). The service life of production lines in manufacturing, with predictive maintenance based on predictive models such as SVM, was studied by Taşcı et al. (2023). Data from buses trucks was measured to accurately forecast engine oil degradation and operational parameters using SVM, Random Forest (RF), and Gaussian process regression by Omiya et al. (2025). Folz Gomes (2025) conducted a comparative analysis between SVM and RF for electric motor state classification using mel-frequency cepstral coefficients as features. Cai et al. (2024) used a Q-learning algorithm with simulated annealing optimization to minimize maintenance costs of continuous process manufacturing systems.

The proposed approach differs from previous work in several key aspects. First, unlike traditional approaches that rely on predefined architectures, the framework automatically discovers optimal neural network designs tailored specifically for vibration forecasting and anomaly detection. Second, incorporate a novel search space that spans both time and frequency domains, enabling the discovery of architectures that optimally leverage spectral features and temporal patterns. Third, implement a multi-faceted anomaly detection strategy that combines reconstruction error, latent space analysis, and temporal pattern assessment to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of machine health.

This study makes the following primary contributions:

- • A novel DNAS-VA specifically designed to discover optimal architectures for vibration forecasting is proposed;
- • A flexible search space incorporating spectral transformations, attention mechanisms, and temporal modeling components, enabling the automated design of architectures that exploit both time-domain and frequency-domain features is evaluated;
- • A FI-VAE model integrating predictive forecasting with probabilistic anomaly detection, enhancing robustness in identifying deviations is proposed;
- • A two-stage optimization strategy based on a hierarchical training protocol that jointly refines neural architecture search (NAS) and anomaly detection performance is approached;
- • A comprehensive evaluation methodology that combines multiple anomaly indicators to improve detection accuracy is evaluated;
- • Empirical validation on real-world vibration data collected from operational machinery is realized, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing approaches.

### 3. Dataset

This study employs time-series vibration data obtained from a sensor mounted on a three-phase synchronous alternating current motor within an industrial facility. The motor, which operates a furnace exhaust fan, is a two-pole unit controlled by a frequency inverter for speed regulation. The vibration sensor is affixed to the motor housing using either adhesive or screws to ensure secure contact and reduce signal interference during data acquisition. Measurements are recorded along three orthogonal axes: X (axial), Y (radial), and Z (radial perpendicular to Y), enabling the detection of mechanical anomalies such as imbalances and excessive vibrations that may precede equipment failure.

Variational autoencoders (VAEs) have been applied in vibration analysis (Lee et al., 2024), offering both dimensionality reduction and generative capabilities. Dabaghi-Zarandi et al. (2025) developed a convolutional VAE to analyze vibration signals from on-load tap changers, mapping them to a 2D latent space for visual inspection and anomaly detection. Tran et al. (2023) developed a fault recognition and correction system for induction motors using deep learning for vibration analysis and classification. Dehghani et al. (2024) employed a deep VAE model for anomaly detection in elevator electromotor systems, demonstrating the model’s capacity to differentiate between normal and faulty operational states through clustering, although limitations were observed under complex conditions. Ibrahim et al. (2024) proposed a reformed VAE for electrical machine fault diagnosis that addressed the nondeterministic nature of VAEs by assigning fixed positions to related faults in a 2D visualization space and adding a desirability function to standardize fault representation. Zhao et al. (2022) presented a normalized VAE with adaptive focal loss for bearing-rotor system fault diagnosis, enhancing feature learning from limited fault data and improving performance across imbalanced categories.

The information about the dataset can be obtained by Borré et al. (2023). Following preprocessing to eliminate null entries, the dataset consists of 7675 records spanning August 2021 to August 2022. Although the intended sampling interval was hourly over a one-year period, operational disruptions resulted in intermittent gaps in data collection. - 5.2. Hierarchical training protocol

Training the integrated FI-VAE involves addressing the distinct optimization objectives of its forecasting and anomaly detection components. A hierarchical training protocol (see details in Fig. 6) is implemented to support convergence and optimization of each subsystem. The approach uses a multi-phase training strategy that alternates between optimizing the forecasting component and the variational autoencoder component. This approach is formulated as:

The hierarchical training protocol consists of three main phases: In the first phase of each epoch, the DARTS-discovered forecasting component is trained. This involves freezing the VAE parameters and optimizing the forecasting-specific loss 𝜆forecast. A decaying teacher forcing ratio 𝑝tf = 𝑝(0)tf ⋅𝛾𝑒 is used, which reduces the reliance on ground truth inputs as training progresses. In the second phase of each epoch, the flow-enhanced variational autoencoder is trained. The forecasting parameters are frozen, and a combined loss function is optimized:

total = forecast + 𝜆vaevae + 𝜆temptemp. (92)

Forecasts are generated by the frozen component before passing to the VAE. After completing the alternating training phases (𝐸total epochs), a dedicated refinement phase for the VAE component is implemented with permanently frozen forecasting model parameters. This extended VAE training for 𝐸refine epochs focuses on anomaly scoring optimization for distinguishing between normal and anomalous vibration patterns.

The hierarchical training protocol offers several characteristics: Component specialization through isolated training of each subsystem; Convergence stability by preventing the simultaneous optimization of interdependent components; The transfer learning effect as the VAE builds upon the forecasting component; Resource allocation by focusing computational resources on one component at a time; Training progress monitoring through phase-specific loss tracking.

This approach allows each component to develop its specialized capabilities while maintaining its integration. The final model combines forecasting with anomaly detection capability for vibration monitoring and predictive maintenance applications.

### 7. Conclusions

![image 23](<32_images/imageFile23.png>)

This paper presented a novel approach to vibration-based predictive maintenance through a zero-cost differentiable architecture search framework that simultaneously addresses forecasting and anomaly detection. The proposed architecture demonstrated efficiency in automatically discovering optimal neural architectures tailored specifically for vibration data, without requiring extensive computational resources traditionally associated with architecture search methods.

The network, discovered through the DARTS methodology, achieved superior forecasting performance across three vibration axes when compared to traditional methods. The multi-domain processing approach, incorporating both wavelet and Fourier components that were automatically selected during architecture search, enabled the model to capture diverse patterns at multiple scales and frequencies without requiring manual engineering of these components. The integration of the FI-VAE provided a robust anomaly detection mechanism that successfully identified potential fault conditions while maintaining a significantly lower false positive rate compared to established baseline methods such as ECOD and Isolation Forest.

Fig. 17. Comparison between the learned latent distribution (solid blue) and the standard normal prior (dashed orange) for dimension 0.

This indicates that the KL divergence term in the VAE loss function effectively regularizes the latent space while still allowing the model to capture the underlying data distribution. The small deviations from perfect normality suggest the model has adapted to represent important variations in the vibration patterns, particularly at the tails of the distribution where anomalies are more likely to occur.

The latent space analysis revealed well-structured representations of machine states, with anomalies appearing primarily at cluster boundaries or as outliers, providing interpretable results that can directly inform maintenance decision-making. A key innovation in the proposed approach was the multi-fidelity evaluation framework that leveraged zero-cost metrics to efficiently filter candidate architectures. The strong correlation between the Fisher Information metric and aggregate performance demonstrated that architectures can be effectively evaluated without the computational burden of full training cycles. The hierarchical training protocol proved effective in balancing the dual objectives of forecasting and anomaly detection, enabling each component to develop specialized capabilities while maintaining its integration.

Fig. 18 shows the temporal evolution of the first three latent dimensions (𝜇0, 𝜇1, and 𝜇2) across a forecast horizon of 5 time steps. The smooth trajectories result from the temporal smoothness constraint in the VAE loss function, ensuring physically consistent predictions and providing a baseline for detecting anomalous temporal patterns.

All three dimensions exhibit smooth trajectories, with 𝜇2 showing the most pronounced variation, starting at approximately −0.14 and gradually increasing to −0.125. This consistency in the latent space is a direct result of the model’s temporal smoothness loss, which penalizes abrupt changes in the latent representations between consecutive time steps. The smooth latent trajectories translate to coherent and physically plausible forecasts, avoiding erratic predictions that would be inconsistent with the mechanical constraints of the system.

This approach not only balanced computational resources but also facilitated knowledge transfer between forecasting and anomaly detection tasks, resulting in a more cohesive predictive maintenance system. Several limitations merit consideration in future work. First, while zero-cost metrics substantially reduce computational requirements, the approach still requires separate phases to train the forecasting and anomaly detection components. Second, the model assumes access to sufficient normal operational data to establish baseline behavior, which may not be available in all industrial settings. Third, although the approach performs well on triaxial vibration data, its effectiveness on other sensor modalities remains to be evaluated.

The smooth temporal evolution of latent variables also facilitates more reliable anomaly detection. When new data deviates significantly from these expected smooth trajectories, it provides a strong signal for potential anomalies. This approach is particularly valuable for the industrial monitoring context, where sudden changes in the internal state representation often precede visible manifestations of mechanical issues in the raw vibration signals.

The temporal smoothness constraints reduced the average step-tostep latent vector L2 distance by 47% compared to an unconstrained model, while the KL divergence between the learned distribution and standard normal achieved a value of 0.089, indicating good alignment with the prior distribution. These properties ensure that the model can effectively detect anomalies while maintaining a well-structured and interpretable latent space.

Future research directions can include extending the framework to incorporate multimodal sensor data beyond vibration measurements, developing online neural architecture search capabilities to adapt to gradual shifts in machine behavior, implementing explainable artificial intelligence techniques to provide maintenance personnel with actionable insights about detected anomalies, and investigating transfer learning approaches to reduce the data requirements for deployment on new machinery types.

- 6.4. Limitations


The proposed method relies on continuous vibration data. In industrial settings, sensors may fail, suffer from noise, or experience data gaps due to maintenance or communication issues, which can degrade model performance. The model is tailored to specific vibration characteristics of a given machine.
