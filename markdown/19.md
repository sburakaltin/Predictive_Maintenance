## H I G H L I G H T S

Modular two-stage framework for anomaly detection on forecasted sensor data, adaptable to diverse industrial applications. Enables proactive failure prediction with adjustable forecast horizons, giving maintenance teams flexible lead times. Supports unsupervised and semi-supervised modes, improving PdM applicability when labeled fault data is scarce.

• Evaluated with 12 anomaly detectors, 3 forecast horizons, and 5 metrics to assess accuracy, robustness, and trade-offs.

Keywords: Early failure detection Time series forecasting Anomaly detection Predictive maintenance

## A B S T R A C T

Early fault detection remains a critical challenge in predictive maintenance (PdM), particularly within critical infrastructure, where undetected failures or delayed interventions can compromise safety and disrupt operations. Traditional anomaly detection methods are typically reactive, relying on real-time sensor data to identify deviations as they occur. This reactive nature often provides insufficient lead time for effective maintenance planning. To address this limitation, we propose a novel two-stage early detection framework that integrates time series forecasting with anomaly detection to anticipate equipment failures several hours in advance. In the first stage, future sensor signal values are predicted using forecasting models; in the second, conventional anomaly detection algorithms are applied directly to the forecasted data. By shifting from real-time to anticipatory detection, the framework aims to deliver actionable early warnings, enabling timely and preventive maintenance.

We validate this approach through a case study focused on metro train systems, an environment where early fault detection is crucial for minimizing service disruptions, optimizing maintenance schedules, and ensuring passenger safety. The framework is evaluated across three forecast horizons (1, 3, and 6 hours ahead) using twelve state-of-the-art anomaly detection algorithms from diverse methodological families. Detection performance is assessed using five performance metrics. Results show that anomaly detection remains highly effective at short to medium horizons, with performance at 1-hour and 3-hour forecasts comparable to that of real-time data. Ensemble and deep learning models exhibit strong robustness to forecast uncertainty, maintaining consistent results with real-time data even at 6-hour forecasts. In contrast, distance- and density-based models suffer substantial degradation at longer horizons (6-hours), reflecting their sensitivity to distributional shifts in predicted signals.

Overall, the proposed framework offers a practical and extensible solution for enhancing traditional PdM systems with proactive capabilities. By enabling early anomaly detection on forecasted data, it supports improved decision-making, operational resilience, and maintenance planning in industrial environments.

Predictive Maintenance (PdM), which seeks to anticipate equipment failures and minimize unexpected disruptions. PdM replaces traditional corrective or preventive maintenance strategies by leveraging datadriven models to predict when maintenance is required, enabling timely and cost-efficient intervention [1].

Recent advancements in machine learning and artificial intelligence have significantly enhanced PdM capabilities [2]. This work focuses on one of its core tasks, failure prediction, which seeks to detect signs of degradation before a failure occurs. Effective solutions to this problem are especially valuable in industry, offering substantial savings in both time and cost. However, many existing PdM systems remain reactive, identifying faults only after deviations from expected behavior have already occurred. Since detection is typically performed on real-time data, such methods often provide little opportunity for proactive planning. This shortcoming is particularly problematic in time-sensitive domains such as rail transport [3], aviation [4], nuclear plant control [5], and critical medical systems [6], where even short delays in detection can lead to major service interruptions, high costs, or serious safety risks [7].

To overcome these limitations, we propose a novel early failure detection framework that leverages real-time time series data collected from sensors and integrates two key research areas: time series forecasting [8] and anomaly detection [9] to achieve early fault identification based on the system’s anticipated future behavior. Our methodology follows a two-stage architecture: in the first stage, a forecasting model predicts future values of real-time signals; in the second stage, an anomaly detection algorithm analyzes these forecasted signals to identify irregular patterns indicative of potential failures. While prior studies have incorporated forecasting within anomaly detection frameworks (typically by comparing predicted and observed values to detect deviations at the current moment) [10], our approach differs in both intent and implementation. Forecasting is not embedded as part of the anomaly detection mechanism itself, but rather serves as an independent and preceding step designed to anticipate future system behavior. This separation, which we term a forecast-then-detect methodology, allows for the detection of failures in advance, thus enabling sufficient lead time for preventive maintenance actions. In our framework, future signal values are first predicted, and then conventional anomaly detection techniques are applied directly to these forecasts to detect failures before they manifest. While both forecasting and anomaly detection are well-established research areas, their integration into a cohesive framework aimed at early fault detection remains underexplored. By combining the forwardlooking capacity of forecasting with the sensitivity of anomaly detection, our proposal delivers a flexible, robust, and proactive strategy for fault management in industrial environments.

To validate the framework, we apply it to the detection of failures in Auxiliary Power Units (APUs) installed on the roofs of metro trains [11]. These units perform essential functions such as regulating vehicle height and ensuring stability which directly influence service reliability and passenger satisfaction. Given that APUs lack redundancy, a failure necessitates the immediate withdrawal of the affected train, causing delays and operational disruptions. Existing maintenance methods often struggle to detect gradual degradation, leading to unexpected failures and higher costs. Previous studies have addressed APU fault detection, though often in a reactive fashion. For instance, Barros et al. [11] developed a real-time classifier based on historical operational data, Davari et al. [12] proposed a hybrid model combining statistical thresholds with machine learning also on real-time signals. Najjar et al. [13] applied recurrent neural networks to capture temporal dependencies in sensor data streams. While these efforts achieve good results on real-time data, none incorporate forecasting to anticipate failures hours before they occur, a key capability offered by our framework. By anticipating APU failures even a few hours in advance, unscheduled removals can be avoided, emergency repair costs reduced, and service continuity preserved.

In our study, we evaluate several configurations of the proposed framework, testing 3 forecasting horizons (1, 3, and 6 hours in advance) and 12 anomaly detection algorithms. Performance is assessed using 5

standard evaluation metrics, capturing both the ability to detect faults and to correctly identify normal system behavior. The results indicate that detection performance on forecasted signals remains largely consistent with real-time detection, particularly at the 1-hour and 3-hour horizons, where most algorithms exhibit robust and reliable behavior when compared to the baseline of real-time anomaly detection. Notably, ensemble-based models such as XGBOD and reconstruction-based models like AutoEncoder and VAE demonstrate high resilience to prediction noise. For instance, XGBOD maintains F1-scores above 0.86 and precision values exceeding 0.88 even at a 6-hour forecast horizon. In contrast, methods based on density estimation or clustering, such as KDE and CBLOF, exhibit substantial degradation in precision and recall as the forecast horizon increases, highlighting their sensitivity to distributional shifts in predicted data. These findings validate the effectiveness of the proposed forecast-then-detect framework for reliably anticipating incipient APU failures, offering sufficient lead time to support meaningful maintenance interventions without significantly compromising detection accuracy. In practice, this enables a strategic shift from reactive to proactive maintenance, improving both service continuity and cost efficiency. While our case study focuses on metro systems, the proposed methodology is broadly applicable to a wide range of realworld industrial environments. The key advantages of the framework include:

- • Flexible and Modular Architecture: We propose a modular and extensible two-stage framework that enables early fault detection by applying anomaly detection techniques to forecasted sensor signals, rather than relying solely on real-time data. By integrating established methodologies from time series forecasting and anomaly detection, the framework facilitates the anticipation of equipment failures. Its flexible design allows for seamless adaptation across diverse industrial environments, supporting the integration or replacement of different methodologies at each stage according to domain-specific requirements.

Moreover, the framework incorporates flexibility through its compatibility with unsupervised and semi-supervised environments, allowing it to operate effectively under varying levels of data labeling. This capability is particularly valuable in industrial contexts where annotated fault data are often scarce, thereby reinforcing both the generality and scalability of the proposed approach.

- • Support for Early Anticipation: The framework supports multiple forecast horizons, offering the flexibility to anticipate failures as far in advance as operational needs require. By adjusting the temporal horizon, maintenance teams can tailor the lead time for preventive actions based on system criticality or planning constraints. Unlike traditional anomaly detection systems that function reactively, our approach is proactive, predicting future system states and detecting anomalies before they manifest. This shift enhances operational foresight and aligns with PdM strategies aimed at minimizing downtime, reducing maintenance costs, and optimizing resource allocation.

Additional contributions of this work include:

- • Real-World Validation: The framework’s utility is demonstrated through a comprehensive case study involving APUs installed in Metro do Porto trains. This real-world application validates the framework under operational conditions, using sensor data, including scenarios where failure labels are unavailable. The study confirms the practical relevance of the framework and its potential for deployment in transportation systems and other critical infrastructure domains.
- • Comprehensive Performance Evaluation: A rigorous experimental evaluation was conducted to assess the feasibility and robustness of the proposed framework. The analysis includes two main components: (1) forecasting performance across three prediction horizons (1 h, 3 h, and 6 h), and (2) anomaly detection effectiveness using


12 state-of-the-art detection algorithms. These algorithms were evaluated across 5 standard performance metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, specificity, and F1-score) capturing both overall and faultspecific detection performance. The study is structured around four key research questions: RQ1 examines the statistical significance of forecast degradation across horizons; RQ2 investigates whether forecasted signals can be effectively used for anomaly detection; RQ3 evaluates the trade-off between detection accuracy and anticipation time; and RQ4 identifies which algorithms are most resilient to forecast uncertainty.

This multi-perspective analysis provides strong empirical support for the framework’s practical viability in early anomaly detection and decision support for PdM applications.

### 2 . Related work

In this section, we provide a concise overview of anomaly detection methods in PdM, with a specific focus on approaches applied to railway systems, which represent our case study. Through this review, we aim to establish the foundation for highlighting the novelty and advantages of our proposed methodology.

Anomaly detection has received considerable research attention in recent years as a key strategy for failure detection in PdM systems [9]. Among the various paradigms, data-driven approaches have become predominant over knowledge-driven ones. While knowledgedriven models rely on domain experts to define abnormal patterns based on empirical knowledge and historical experience, data-driven methods exploit large volumes of sensor data, applying statistical analysis and machine learning to detect or predict faults in industrial environments. Currently, data-driven anomaly detection typically addresses two critical challenges [14]: the limited availability of labeled data and the timing of anomaly detection. Depending on the availability of labeled data, methods are commonly classified into supervised, semi-supervised, and unsupervised categories. Although supervised models have demonstrated high accuracy in domains such as gear condition monitoring [15] and gearbox fault detection [16], their dependence on large volumes of labeled data significantly limits their applicability in real-world PdM scenarios, where annotated fault instances are often scarce or unavailable. As a result, there is a growing emphasis on semi-supervised and unsupervised approaches, which are more adaptable to data-limited environments. In terms of detection timing, most existing models focus on identifying whether the system is currently operating under abnormal conditions [17,18]. While effective for real-time fault detection, these methods provide little or no anticipatory capability, as they typically recognize anomalies only when deviations are already occurring. Consequently, there is increasing interest in methodologies that facilitate early fault detection (before the onset of observable failures) as these are more aligned with the goals of PdM and the operational demands of high-reliability systems.

Focusing on semi-supervised and unsupervised approaches, these models avoid the dependency on labeled fault data, an advantage in many real-world industrial settings where annotations are scarce. In both cases, anomalies are identified as data sequences that diverge significantly from learned patterns, whether in terms of statistical similarity, temporal prediction, or latent representations. Unsupervised methods operate on unlabeled datasets containing both normal and faulty sequences, with faults typically representing a minority class. In contrast, semi-supervised models are trained exclusively on normal data and are designed to detect deviations from this baseline behavior. Among the different methodological families, techniques based on distance metrics, temporal prediction and reconstruction have been widely explored. Reconstruction-based methods, such as autoencoders

[19] have gained prominence. These models encode normal patterns and use reconstruction errors to flag anomalous behavior. Similarly, prediction-based methods, including LSTM-based architectures [20,21], use forecasting to compare observed values against expected ones. In this paradigm, deviations between predicted and actual signals serve as implicit indicators of potential faults. Although these models have shown strong performance in fault detection, they are applied in real-time contexts, limiting their utility to reactive interventions once the deviation has already occurred. In contrast, our approach introduces a forecastingthen-detection methodology in which forecasting is decoupled from the anomaly detection algorithm and instead serves as a central and independent preliminary step. By predicting future signal values, this forecasting stage enables early fault anticipation. Subsequently, anomaly detection algorithms are applied to the forecasted data to identify potential failures. This enables earlier fault detection and provides a valuable lead time for maintenance planning and proactive decision-making. Beyond reconstruction and prediction-based methods, other widely used unsupervised techniques include distance- and density-based models such as Local Outlier Factor and Isolation Forest, which leverage nearestneighbor relations to flag outliers. Encoding-based methods also play a role by applying transformation techniques to highlight rare or unexpected patterns in the data [22–24]. Additionally, statistical approaches [25] have been employed for their interpretability, although they often struggle to adapt to the complexity and variability of real-world sensor dynamics.

In the railway domain, various anomaly detection approaches have been explored, particularly those based on reconstruction. A common strategy involves learning normal system behavior by encoding segments of time series data into a lower-dimensional latent space and calculating anomaly scores based on reconstruction errors. Several studies have applied this technique in railway environments. For example, Chen et al. [26] proposed a semi-supervised GAN-based method for detecting foreign objects on railway tracks. Kang et al. [27] developed a CNN-based system to identify surface defects on railway insulators, while Kang et al. [28] introduced an LSTM autoencoder for anomaly detection in subway brake operating units. Beyond reconstruction, other railway-related studies have adopted prediction, distance-based, or statistical approaches. Kang et al. [29] utilized a Bayesian statistical model to detect anomalies in train speed. Manco et al. [30] designed a fault diagnosis application to explain metro train door failures. Filios et al. [31] employed ARIMA and Prophet to predict stoppages in packaging machinery. Baptista et al. [32] combined the ARMA algorithm with machine learning models such as K-Nearest Neighbors, Random Forest, and Support Vector Machine to forecast failure events. Additionally, Zhang et al. [33] constructed a graphical model to detect abnormal passenger flow. In contrast, Shi et al. [34] proposed a density peak clustering approach to identify frequent failures in rail vehicle door systems.

Notably, several studies have worked with the same MetroPT dataset used in our case study. Barros et al. [11] developed heuristic rules for anomaly detection in APUs using peak frequency analysis. Davari et al. [12] implemented a deep learning-based unsupervised PdM framework leveraging Sparse Autoencoders to reduce false alarms. Ayat et al. [13] applied supervised learning through a Random Forest model to detect and classify APU failures.

In contrast to prior approaches that operate directly on current signals, our framework introduces an explicit modular separation between forecasting and detection. This design anticipates potential faults before they manifest, providing a valuable time window for maintenance planning and intervention. Furthermore, the modular structure enables independent selection, optimization, and replacement of models in each stage, enhancing adaptability across diverse operational contexts and learning settings. Such flexibility makes the framework particularly suitable for industrial environments with scarce labeled data, underscoring its novelty and practical relevance for real-world PdM scenarios that demand early fault anticipation and reliable decision-making.

### 3 . Proposed framework

This section presents the overall architecture of the proposed framework for early fault detection, as illustrated in Fig. 1. The framework follows a two-step process grounded in a forecasting-then-detection methodology. In the first step, real-time sensor data is processed by a forecasting model to predict the future behavior of sensor signals across several time horizons (in our experiments: one, three, and six hours ahead, although this interval can be flexibly adapted to the specific application domain). In the second step, these forecasted signals are analyzed by an anomaly detection model to detect future anomalous behavior. By introducing forecasting as a preliminary stage, the architecture supports anticipatory diagnostics and facilitates timely maintenance actions through a simple yet effective design. Notably, the modular nature of the framework allows for the integration of established techniques from both time series forecasting and anomaly detection, ensuring flexibility, scalability, and robustness. The following sections describe the core components of the proposed framework in detail.

- 3.1. Time series forecasting as the first step for our early anomaly detection framework


The first phase of the proposed fault detection framework is centered on time series forecasting, which plays a crucial role in enabling early identification of anomalies. Traditional anomaly detection techniques typically operate on real-time sensor data, identifying faults only once they begin to manifest. However, in the context of PdM, early detection is critical, as it allows sufficient lead time to schedule interventions and avoid unexpected downtime or system failures. If anomalies are detected only at the current moment, the time available to take corrective action is drastically reduced, limiting the operational value of the detection.

To address this limitation, our approach introduces a forecastingthen-detection paradigm. Instead of evaluating anomalies based on real-time signals, we first predict the future behavior of the system using multivariate time series forecasting models. The predicted signals corresponding to future time steps (for example, 1, 3, and 6 hours ahead) are then analyzed by an anomaly detection model. This architecture transforms the detection task into a proactive process, effectively extending the decision-making window by the length of the forecast horizon. As illustrated in Fig. 2, forecasting future signal behavior enables anomaly detection models to identify potential faults in advance, thereby providing actionable lead time for preventive interventions.

While previous studies have employed forecasting techniques within anomaly detection frameworks, typically under the forecasting-based anomaly detection paradigm, their application fundamentally differs from our proposal. In those approaches [35,36], forecasting is primarily used to reconstruct expected signal behavior, and anomalies are inferred

by comparing predicted and observed values at the current time. In contrast, our framework explicitly decouples prediction and detection into two distinct, sequential stages. This design offers several key advantages:

- • Integration of state-of-the-art forecasting models: The framework leverages advanced time series forecasting methods to generate accurate and robust signal estimates, improving the reliability of subsequent detection.
- • Modular anomaly detection on predicted data: Any state-of-the-art anomaly detection algorithm can be applied to the forecasted signals, enabling the system to anticipate potential faults using existing techniques, rather than detecting them only in real time.
- • Explicit control over forecast horizon: The architecture allows domain experts to define how far in advance faults should be identified in alignment with operational and maintenance requirements.


To complete the first stage of our proposed framework, we define the time series forecasting task as applied in this context. The objective of this phase is to generate predicted signals for future time points, which will subsequently serve as input for the anomaly detection stage. Time series forecasting involves predicting future values of a sequence based on historical observations. Formally, let 𝑥1,𝑥2,…,𝑥𝑡 denote a univariate or multivariate time series, where 𝑥𝑡 is the value observed at time 𝑡. Given a forecasting horizon ℎ, the goal is to learn a function 𝑓 that maps the past observations 𝑥1,𝑥2,…,𝑥𝑡 to predicted values 𝑥̂𝑡 + ℎ, minimizing the error between 𝑥̂𝑡 + ℎ and the actual future value 𝑥𝑡+ℎ.

For the forecasting phase, we employ a hybrid deep learning model that combines CNN and LSTM architectures to process multiple input signals and independently predict each output variable. Similar hybrid architectures have proven effective in various domains [37], where the CNN component extracts local signal features and the LSTM blocks capture long-term temporal dependencies, enabling accurate forecasting of the system’s expected behavior across different time horizons. Dropout regularization is applied to reduce overfitting. Following the findings of a previous study [38], where several forecasting models were evaluated and the CNN–LSTM hybrid consistently demonstrated the most robust and representative performance, this architecture was selected for the present work. Nevertheless, the model remains modular and flexible, allowing the integration of alternative forecasting approaches to ensure adaptability across diverse domains and data characteristics.

Our model consists of a sequence of a convolutional layer and LSTM blocks, which are integrated using a fully connected layer to generate the final output. Initially, the input data is passed through a one-dimensional convolutional layer, which extracts features and produces a two-dimensional output vector with 32 filters and a kernel size of 2. - 3.2. Anomaly detection as second step for our early anomaly detection framework


The second phase of the proposed framework focuses on detecting anomalies in the predicted signals generated during the forecasting stage. The core idea is to enable the use of any existing anomaly detection technique as a modular component within a flexible and generalizable early fault detection architecture. This design supports the integration of supervised, unsupervised, or semi-supervised models, depending on the availability of labeled data. Fig. 4 illustrates how the detection algorithm is applied across different time horizons, starting from the future signals. The flexibility of the framework allows any anomaly detection model to be utilized.

In real-world industrial environments, data labeling is often scarce or incomplete. As a result, our approach prioritizes the use of semisupervised and unsupervised anomaly detection methods, which are more broadly applicable under such constraints. These methods either require only normal (i.e., fault-free) data or can operate effectively with partially labeled datasets, without the need for explicit annotation of failure types. To demonstrate the flexibility and robustness of the proposed framework, we conduct a comparative study involving a diverse set of anomaly detection algorithms, all applied to forecasted signal sequences. The framework’s modular design allows for the integration of any compatible detection model, provided it can process the output generated by the forecasting stage.

The selected algorithms encompass a variety of theoretical foundations, including probabilistic modeling, distance-based outlier scoring,

ensemble learning, and reconstruction. The following categories summarize the employed techniques:

- • Statistical and Density-Based Models. These models assume an underlying probability distribution and identify anomalies as deviations from expected statistical behavior: the Minimum Covariance Determinant (MCD) algorithm [39] is based on robust covariance estimators and is particularly effective for identifying multivariate outliers. The Copula-Based Outlier Detection (COPOD) method [25] leverages copula theory to estimate reference distributions in a nonparametric manner, enabling the detection of anomalies without strong distributional assumptions. Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) [40] identifies anomalies by estimating the probability density function of the data; points located in low-density regions are flagged as outliers. The Empirical Cumulative Outlier Detection (ECOD) method [41] is a non-parametric approach that relies on the empirical cumulative distribution function to identify anomalous points without requiring prior assumptions about data distribution. Lastly, the Histogram-Based Outlier Score (HBOS) method [42] offers a fast and scalable solution by constructing histograms for each dimension independently, making it particularly suitable for high-volume datasets.
- • Proximity-Based Models. These models detect anomalies by measuring the distance or proximity differences among data points: Clustering-Based Local Outlier Factor (CBLOF) algorithm [43] incorporates clustering techniques to evaluate how far a given point lies from its assigned cluster, thereby classifying distant points as anomalies.


- • Ensemble-Based Models. These models combine the outputs of multiple detectors to enhance robustness and overall performance: Isolation Forest (IForest) [44] is a widely used ensemble method that isolates anomalies instead of profiling normal data. It builds random trees and measures the path length required to isolate a data point, with shorter paths indicating potential anomalies. In contrast, Extreme Gradient Boosting Outlier Detection (XGBOD) [45] adopts a semi-supervised ensemble approach by integrating unsupervised feature generation with the XGBoost algorithm, enabling effective anomaly detection even in the presence of limited labeled data. Finally, the Locally Selective Combination in Parallel (LSCP) [46] dynamically selects the most appropriate base detectors for each instance based on local data characteristics, improving accuracy through context-aware ensemble selection.
- • Reconstruction-Based Models. These neural network architectures are designed to learn compact representations of normal data and reconstruct the original input. Instances that yield high reconstruction errors are flagged as anomalies. The AutoEncoder (AE) [47], is a feedforward neural network that compresses and reconstructs input signals; reconstruction errors are used as anomaly scores. The Variational AutoEncoder (VAE) [48] extends this approach by introducing a probabilistic latent space, allowing the model to capture the underlying distribution of the data. VAE enables robust modeling of complex patterns, making it particularly effective for detecting subtle or distributed anomalies.
- • Generative Models. These approaches learn to generate realistic samples and identify deviations using adversarial training: SingleObjective Generative Adversarial Active Learning (SO-GAAL) [49] employs a generative adversarial framework to synthesize informative samples, thereby improving the representation of the decision boundary between normal and anomalous instances. This approach is particularly effective in capturing complex data distributions where traditional methods may struggle.

By incorporating traditional statistical techniques, deep learning architectures, and adversarial approaches, the study spans multiple algorithmic paradigms, ensuring broad applicability. The objective is to establish its practical value as a PdM tool and as a decision support system in industrial environments. ### 4 . Case study: Fault in air production unit in metro trains

To demonstrate the practical relevance and technical efficacy of the proposed framework for early failure detection, this study conducts a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The objective is to assess whether it is possible not only to detect equipment failures reliably but also to anticipate them early enough to enable proactive maintenance planning. To that end, we present a real-world case study involving fault detection in the APU of metro trains, a critical subsystem responsible for supplying compressed air to essential onboard functions. The case study includes a detailed problem description, experimental setup, and a thorough analysis of results, offering a realistic scenario to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed approach under real operational conditions.

4.1. Study case description

The MetroPT dataset [50,51] contains information collected from the urban metro public transportation service in Porto, Portugal. It serves as a benchmark for PdM models, encompassing tasks related to online anomaly detection, failure prediction, and remaining useful life prediction. The dataset collects data from a range of analog and digital sensors installed in the APU of a train, enabling the detection of real-time changes in its performance. The APU is a vital component of the compressor, responsible for converting power from an electric motor into kinetic energy by compressing and pressurizing air. Its performance is crucial for various actions such as braking, door opening and closing, and bioreactor usage. The dataset used in this paper was collected from February to July 2020 and includes 16 signals logged at a frequency of 1 Hz, with data transmitted to the remote server every five minutes using the GSM network.

4.4. Experimental results

In this section, we present the experimental study conducted to address the RQs posed in this work. The main objective is to demonstrate that the proposed framework effectively enables the early detection of faults through time series forecasting combined with anomaly detection. To this end, we analyze the performance of the framework in its two sequential phases. - 4.3.2. Metrics for anomaly detection In the anomaly detection stage, the objective is to correctly identify


the presence or absence of anomalous behavior associated with failures. Given the binary nature of this task (failure vs. no failure), standard classification metrics are employed.

Based on recent literature in anomaly detection and fault diagnosis [54], we adopt point-based metrics rather than event-based metrics. In point-based evaluation, each timestamp is assessed individually as either anomalous or normal, treating anomalies as discrete points rather than contiguous time intervals (events). This approach was selected for our application for several reasons. First, it enables a more granular evaluation of the model’s ability to correctly identify all timestamps involved in a fault. Second, point-based metrics are less sensitive to the precise definition of event boundaries, which often vary across studies and may introduce bias by favoring certain methods over others. Third, it allows for a direct comparison between predicted and actual anomalies at every time step, facilitating detailed analysis of detection performance, including false alarms and missed detections. Since our objective is to assess how effectively the detection method identifies individual instances of anomalous behavior, point-based evaluation provides a more informative and flexible framework.

### 5 . Conclusions and future work

This study demonstrates the feasibility and effectiveness of a novel two-stage framework for anticipatory anomaly detection. By combining time series forecasting with traditional anomaly detection techniques, the framework shifts detection from a reactive to a proactive paradigm. The proposed approach was validated through a real-world case study involving metro train systems, where early fault identification is critical for ensuring operational continuity and passenger safety. The experimental results demonstrate that anomaly detection remains highly reliable when applied to forecasted signals, particularly at shorter horizons (+1-hour and +3-hours), where performance across different metrics is comparable to that achieved using real-time data, which serves as the baseline. Even at extended forecast horizons (+6-hours), robust algorithms like XGBOD, AE, and ECOD maintain performance levels close to those achieved on real-time data, with minimal degradation in precision and recall, underscoring their robustness to forecasting-induced noise. These findings confirm that early warnings can be generated with sufficient lead time to enable proactive maintenance actions, without incurring a significant loss in detection performance. Importantly, the integration of two research domains, time series forecasting and anomaly detection, has resulted in a modular and flexible framework that can be readily adapted to a variety of industrial scenarios. This flexibility allows for the tailored selection of forecasting models, anomaly detection algorithms, and forecast horizons, aligning the system with the specific operational needs of different application domains.

This study also highlights new challenges. While accuracy and specificity remain stable across forecast horizons, precision and recall (particularly precision) tend to degrade as the forecast window increases. This degradation is most pronounced at the +6-hours horizon and is especially severe for certain algorithms, particularly densityand proximity-based models, which become increasingly prone to false positives. Consequently, most models exhibit a trade-off between early anticipation and detection reliability—an issue that is present even in real-time detection and naturally persists when using forecasted signals. Although false positives may be less harmful than missed detections in critical environments, they can still lead to operational disruptions and loss of trust in the system. Therefore, future work will build on the proposed framework by leveraging its flexibility to develop new detection methods that can operate effectively under forecast uncertainty while achieving a better balance between precision and recall. Based on the results obtained, we propose strengthening the framework by improving Phase 1 through modeling inter-signal dependencies and enhancing time series forecasting to reduce prediction errors and increase detection accuracy. For Phase 2, we propose developing hybrid models that combine the strengths of ensemble learning and deep reconstruction approaches to better handle uncertainty and reduce false positives.
