# A novel unsupervised method for anomaly detection in time series based on statistical features for industrial predictive maintenance

Jesimar da Silva Arantes1 · Márcio da Silva Arantes1 · Herberth Birck Fröhlich2 · Laure Siret3 · Renan Bonnard1

Received: 26 April 2021 / Accepted: 19 August 2021 / Published online: 12 September 2021 © The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer Nature Switzerland AG 2021

Abstract

Industrial production processes are increasingly collecting data from machines in operation due to the cost reduction and popularization of sensor technologies. Valuable information is generated by using the right sensors and proper techniques on the machine’s current state in operation. This extraction allows detecting whether the machine is operating in a degraded state and then, if necessary, interrupts its operation before it goes into a broken state. The detection of the abnormal behavior of a machine is relevant since detection at the right time can reduce ﬁnancial costs due to machine breakdown and production downtime. This work proposes a novel unsupervised method to detect anomalies in industrial machines and interrupt their operation before this machine goes into a state of breakdown. The proposed method receives as input a set of time series data from several sensors. Using a collection of statistical features, it calculates an indicator that characterizes the failure’s severity, issuing an alert to the machine operator if necessary. The method was evaluated in two benchmarks with known univariate data and two proprietary datasets with multivariate data. Conducted experiments revealed the low computational time spent on training and on evaluation. Overall results measured in Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUC) in Yahoo’s benchmark were 89.3%; in Numenta, it was 70.3%, and in the two multivariate datasets evaluated, it was 92.4% and 91.2%. These high AUC values reveal the potential of the proposed method applied in predictive maintenance in a large soybean oil production industry.

Keywords Anomaly detection · Outlier detection · Time series · Feature extraction · Predictive maintenance · Industry 4.0

###### 1 Introduction

Industry4.0revolutionoccurredmainlyduetotheincreasein computational power, cheapening of sensoring technologies, the evolution of Artiﬁcial Intelligence (AI) methods, and the fusion between the physical and digital world [15]. The 4.0 industry brings a series of advantages, such as increased productivity, cheaper production, increased interoperability, lower maintenance costs, and easier interaction between man and machine [20]. This work deals with the aspects related to reducing maintenance costs by developing a novel unsupervised method of anomaly detection in time series, aiming to assist predictive maintenance.

Many authors in the literature do not make a clear distinction between anomalies and outliers, as highlighted by [6]. An outlier is considered an abrupt change at a point that differs from its neighbors in the context of this work, possibly coming from some error in sensing or other error in the data entry. In turn, an anomaly is a change in a set of consecutive

points that can lead a machine to a state of failure or breakage. Our novel method is capable of detecting anomalies and outliers. The method also makes it possible to distinguish local and global outliers. An outlier is local when it evades the pattern established by its neighbors. An outlier is global when it avoids the pattern concerning the entire data series [6].

As mentioned above, the occurrence of an anomaly is the expression of a deviation in the expected behavior of a machine. In turn, anomaly detection consists of the use of techniques that ﬁnd these deviations in data series. There are two main ways of classifying anomaly detection methods: detection in temporal data and spatial data. The present work focuses on the detection of anomalies in temporal data (sequential). An outlier is a point that differs drastically from all the others, i.e., far away from the behavior considered standard. These points that differ from the others are often associated with sensing errors or errors in data entry. Our novel method can be applied to both anomaly detection and outlier detection, just by triggering the statistical features speciﬁc to each context.

Regarding predictive maintenance, the literature presents this subject with two main parts: diagnosis and prognosis. A diagnosis ﬁrst detects, determines the causes, and then evaluates the machine’s health status or system to prevent failures. A prognostic makes predictions about the machine’s futurestateorsystemandpredictstheRemaining UsefulLife (RUL) [13]. The method proposed in this work focuses only on the fault diagnosis stage.

A maintenance procedure is based on four criteria: safety, economic cost, ease, and accuracy [5]. To ensure an optimal meet of these criteria, several types of maintenance strategies exist. Maintenance strategies are often classiﬁed into corrective, preventive (systematic and conditional), and predictive, as reported in [14,27]. The Corrective Maintenance (CrM) strategy is the simplest, as it only occurs when the machine has stopped working. CrM policies are economically advantageous for easy and short interventions. However, CrM policiesarecostlywhenappliedtoexpensivesystemsorcomplex interventions due to the costs generated by downtime and new components. Systematic strategies are time-based only maintenance strategies. Conditional methods are only based on the machine condition, but the conditions’ control and measurement can be done periodically. Preventive Maintenance (PvM) is a better strategy than CrM only if applied with the appropriate frequency. The main drawback of PvM is the unnecessary corrective actions performed due to a misunderstanding of the system, leading to an increasing in operational costs. Predictive Maintenance (PdM) is deﬁned as an extension of PvM [13]. PdM uses predictive toolstoestimatewhenmaintenanceactionswillbenecessary. Strategies of this type of maintenance involve continuous machine monitoring, allowing maintenance to be performed

onlywhennecessary.Ifwellapplied,thePdMstrategybrings ﬁnancial savings since it reduces the number of breakdowns and improves survival duration to mechanical components [25]. The main drawback of PdM is the need for accurate data and good maturity of the system of information of the company [22].

The proposed method was called Anomaly and Outlier Detection (AOD), which is based on a set of statistical features that deﬁne acceptable limits for several variables that support univariate (coming from only one sensor) or multivariate (coming from more than one sensor) data. AOD aims to indicate anomalous behaviors that would possibly lead the machine to a maintenance need. That is an intrinsic need of industries since, as reported by [26], predictive maintenance can increase return on investment by up to 10 times, reduce maintenance costs from 25 to 30%, eliminate breakdowns from 70 to 75%, reduce downtime from 35 to 45%, and increase production from 20 to 25%.

This ﬁrst step of diagnosis performed by AOD provides a unique way to reference different machine failures. Our method can replace or automatically complete fault trees or other failure identiﬁcation techniques that are a prerequisite for any prognostic method. In addition, AOD also allows the identiﬁcation of critical equipment states. A critical state is a state in which the equipment is not in a failure mode but in a state which a quality defect is observable in production. Identifying these states would improve the performance of maintenance intervention prediction during the prognostic step and thus improve the management of production quality.

The AOD method has been evaluated in two benchmarks with univariate data well known in the literature. The ﬁrst benchmark evaluated is the Yahoo Outlier Benchmark (YOB), speciﬁc for detecting outliers in univariate time series [17]. The second dataset is the Numenta Anomaly Benchmark (NAB), typical for detecting anomalies in univariatetimeseries[1,19].Bothdatasetscontainbothartiﬁcial and real anomaly data. Actual anomalies are from various application ﬁelds, including Yahoo member login data, data stream, Amazon Web Services (AWS) server metrics, tweets volume, advertising click metrics, trafﬁc data, and data from machines in operation. The evaluation methods took place in a proprietary dataset with synthetically generated multivariate data and on an actual case study with multivariate data from an industry in the vegetal oil production sector.

This article is organized as follows: Sect. 2 presents a brief literature review. Section 3 presents a description of the problem. Section 4 details the proposed method. Section 5 presents the computational results obtained. Finally, Sect. 6 closes this article with the ﬁnal considerations.

###### 2 Related works

Thereareseveralapproachesintheliteraturetodetectanomalies and outliers in time series. In the context of this work, we are interested in unsupervised techniques, as data labels are not always available for training in real applications. Hence, only Machine Learning (ML) and statistical methods will be considered in this review.

The authors in [7] present a literature review on ML techniques applied in predictive maintenance (PdM). This work shows that the main methods used in PdM, among others, are deep learning, greedy methods, and Bayesian methods. This work also states that these techniques can be applied to many types of equipment, such as turbines, engines, compressors, pumps, and fans. Another aspect raised by the work concerns the data used by the methods, where there is a preference for vibration data, acoustic signal, equipment temperature, electrical signal, and maintenance history.

The work of [21] presents the DeepAnT method that uses a predictor module of the series that uses Convolution Neural Network (CNN) and a module to detect anomalies, responsible for labeling the data as normal or abnormal. This unsupervised method learns the distribution of data and uses this information to predict the future point’s behavior in a time series, and can be used on univariate or multivariate time series.

Still in the strategy of predicting future points to verify if the query point belongs to the expected distribution, the authors in [18] proposed the method EGADS, acronym for Extensible Generic Anomaly Detection System. EGADS performs the detection of outliers and detects points of change in univariate time series, and allows new methods to be added in each of these modules due to the way it was designed. This method has three main parts: a time series modeling module, an anomaly detection module, and an alert issuing module. This work has been validated over Yahoo data, with login data from members of that company.

In [12], the authors present the method S-H-ESD, an acronym for Seasonal Hybrid Extreme Studentized Deviate, which performs the detection of anomalies in univariate time series. S-H-ESD is a statistical method based on hypothesis testing and decomposition of time series into seasonality, trend, and noise. This work has been validated on Twitter data such as tweets per second and CPU utilization.

This work differs from the approaches of [12,18,21] because it makes the detection of anomalies and outliers based on the current analysis window and not on each data point. Still, the works of [12,18] are speciﬁc approaches to univariate time series.

The authors in [29] developed a method based on Hierarchical Temporal Memory (HTM) for anomaly detection in time series. The authors in [10] developed a technique basedontheLongShort-TermMemorynetwork(LSTM)and Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM).

The output of interest of a sensored machine is timespaced data, characterizing a time series. Thus, this series, s, is deﬁned as a sequence of measurements of a variable

- at different points in time and described mathematically in Eq. (1).


###### s = [(x1,t1),(x2,t2),(x3,t3),...,(xN,tN)] (1)

This series is composed of a collection of ordered pairs (xr,tr), where xr represents the value of the series at the instant of time tr. In this series, the value N represents the total amount of data, so r ∈ {1,2,3,... , N}. One of the characteristics of time series is that the value tr is strictly incremental, that is, if r1 < r2 implies that tr1 < tr2. The vast majority of time series models consider t = tr − tr−1 constant, i.e., the time interval between any two samples is always the same. The method proposed in this work also considers t constant, and therefore, this same time series can be written as s = [x1, x2, x3,..., xN]. This work will manipulate several time series, each coming from a different sensor, so it is necessary to distinguish the data series of a i sensor, as shown in Eq. (2).

###### si = [xi,1, xi,2, xi,3,..., xi,n] ∀i ∈ {1,2,3,... , F} (2)

Still, about Eq. (2), there is a total of F sensors in a given machine. Most of the times, different sensors have different acquisition frequencies, that is, different values of t. Data from all sensors were preprocessed to ensure that t would be the same for all of them. Such a procedure usually leads to a reduction in the dataset size but brings a more coherent analysis. However, although performed, the synchronization methodswerenotimplementedorwerenotneededinspeciﬁc benchmarks.

This way of modeling simpliﬁes both the model and the problem description. For example, Eq. (2) will not need an index to identify which machine belongs to the time series.

###### 4 Methods

This method uses a set of statistical features to synthesize the variation limits of an input signal. There are other possibilities for feature extraction based on deep learning, suchasone-dimensionalconvolutionalneuralnetworks.This

approach for addressing data features was considered at ﬁrst, but the large volume of data and the frequent need to update the models would make in-plant processing unfeasible. Therefore, this extraction by statistical variables directly proved to be an active and reasonable alternative to deep learning. These statistical features can be separated into groups, representing central tendency measures, dispersion, coefﬁcients in general, and its derivatives. These groups are the same as mentioned in Table 2. Some of these features have the same unit of measurement as the input data, others have no measurement units, and others have a different unit than the input. Consider the following terminology:

###### 4.1 Statistical features

A central tendency measure is a value that represents the idea of central value in a data set. The method developed supports a total of 10 central tendency features that may be selected or not: Arithmetic Mean (AMean), Root Mean Square (RMS), Mean Value Absolute (MVA), Square Root of Amplitude (SRA), MidRange (MidR), Median, MidHinge (MidH), TriMean, InterQuartile Mean (IQM), and Truncated Mean (TMean).

A measure of dispersion is a value that represents the idea of how elongated or narrow the distribution of a data set is. A total of 8 features of dispersion may be selected: Standard Deviation (STD), Variance (VAR), Peak–Peak Value (PPV),CoefﬁcientofVariation(CV),MedianAbsoluteDeviation (MAD), InterQuartile Range (IQR), InterDecile Range (IDR), and Quartile Coefﬁcient of Dispersion (QCD).

In general, a coefﬁcient measure is a measure of form, an index,orevenameasureofextremesofadataset.Themethod developed supports a total of 16 features of coefﬁcients that may be selected, being them: Skewness Value (SV), Kurtosis Value (KV), Crest Factor (CF), Impulse Factor (IF), Margin Factor (MF), Shape Factor (SF), Kurtosis Factor (KF), Minimum Value (MinV), Maximum Value (MaxV), Skewness Value-Based Quartile (SVBQ), Kurtosis Value-Based Octile (KVBO), Coefﬁcient of Variation in the Quartile (CVQ), ﬁrst quartile (quartile1), third quartile (quartile3), ﬁrst decile (decile1), and ninth decile (decile9).

Finally, a measure in the derivative domain is one of the above features calculated on the series after applying discrete derivative. The method developed supports a total of 34 features in the derivative domain. Some of the supported measures are Derivative of Arithmetic Mean (DAMean), Derivative of STD (DSTD), Derivative of SV (DSV), Derivative of KV (DKV), Derivative of Median (DMedian), Derivative of IQR (DIQR), Derivative of SVBQ (DSVBQ), and Derivative of KVBO (DKVBO).

AOD, like many artiﬁcial intelligence techniques, has two stages, training and testing. Since the proposed method is unsupervised, it is not necessary to have the observation labels. The only requirement during training is that the data series come from a machine under normal operating conditions. If there are data that represent anomalies or outliers, these must be removed, causing the algorithm to learn only the machine’s regular operation. A strategy to remove any anomaliesfromthetrainingsethasalsobeendeveloped since it is impossible to guarantee that the training’s input data are related to normal operating conditions. Regarding the number of observations, the higher the volume of data in the training set, the better. Nevertheless, the results were considered of good quality, even with little data in the training set. A sliding window is used to deﬁne the features extraction region and subsequent analysis during testing. The justiﬁcation for using a data sliding window is because the window allows the calculation of a set of features that require multiple data. Also, the window reveals the signal characteristic better than the techniques that consider only one data point

Algorithm 3 shows the idea behind the evaluation method to identify a possible anomaly or outlier in the input data. An important aspect related to the detection of anomalies is the reduction in noise in the data. For this purpose, the need arises not only to select robust features but also to work with sliding windows. The anomaly or outlier detection is then performed for the window in question and not at every point of the data that make up that window. When pointing, for example, that a particular window contains an outlier, the method does not highlight at which point of the window the outlier is but signals the window as a whole.

The time complexity of the evaluation phase of the algorithm is given by O(F · J ·N ·log(N)). Thus, the evaluation’s complexityissimilartothecomplexityofthetraining,except for the absence of the number of windows factor (K). This point is justiﬁed because the evaluation is done only for the current window. The fact that the complexities, both in training and evaluation, are almost linear implies that the method performs the training and the evaluation very quickly. The typical values of the parameters K, F, J and N of AOD are: K[10,10000], F[1,20], J[4,20], and N[5,500].

###### 6 Discussion and conclusions

###### This paper presented a new method, called Anomaly and Outlier Detector (AOD) for detecting anomalies and out-

The method is designed to run in real time (online) but can also run ofﬂine. It supports univariate or multivariate data evaluation and uses a data sliding window from which statistical features are extracted and analyzed. Due to the way the method was developed, it can easily detect anomalies or outliers just by changing the statistical indicators used. This functionality is critical, since it extends the detection capability of the proposed method to two slightly different domains.
